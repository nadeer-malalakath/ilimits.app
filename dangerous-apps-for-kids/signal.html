What This App Does

A clear picture of Signal — the most privacy-focused mainstream messaging app available, used by journalists, activists, security researchers, and privacy-conscious adults for entirely legitimate communication. Its restriction in iLimits is not about the platform being harmful — Signal is widely regarded as one of the most responsibly designed apps in existence. The concern is narrow and specific: Signal's privacy architecture, deliberately designed to leave no accessible message record, makes it the most effective tool available for a child who specifically wants communication their parents cannot see.

🔐
Signal Is Widely Considered the Gold Standard of Private Messaging — and That Same Privacy Architecture Is What Creates the Parental Oversight Concern
Signal is a messaging application developed by the Signal Foundation, a non-profit organisation committed to private communication as a fundamental right. It is recommended by security experts, journalists, human rights organisations, and privacy researchers worldwide as the most secure and trustworthy consumer messaging platform available. Every message is end-to-end encrypted by default — unlike Telegram, which requires manual selection of Secret Chat mode. Signal has no advertising, no data collection, and deliberately minimal metadata retention. Disappearing messages are available with configurable timers on every conversation. These features represent responsible and principled privacy design in an adult context. In a child's hands, on a device a parent is responsible for, they represent the most complete parental oversight gap available through any mainstream messaging application.

End-to-End Encryption by Default on Every Message — No Action Required
Unlike Telegram, which requires manually switching to Secret Chat mode for end-to-end encryption, Signal encrypts every message end-to-end by default without any user action. There is no standard mode and a secret mode — all Signal messages are end-to-end encrypted from the first message in any conversation. This means that every conversation a child has on Signal — with any contact, about any topic — is encrypted in a way that prevents access by Signal's servers, by the network, or by any monitoring tool. The default nature of the encryption means that even a child who does not understand encryption is automatically using end-to-end encrypted communication simply by using Signal.
⏱️
Disappearing Messages on Every Conversation — Configurable Down to 30 Seconds
Signal allows disappearing message timers to be set on any conversation, with options ranging from 30 seconds to four weeks. When a disappearing message timer is active, messages are automatically deleted from both devices after the configured time expires — with no record remaining on either device or any server. Unlike Telegram's self-destruct feature which must be selected per session, Signal's disappearing message timer can be set as the default for a specific conversation, automatically applying to every message in that thread. A child can configure every Signal conversation to delete messages after 30 seconds, leaving no record of any communication that occurred in the conversation.
🗄️
Minimal Metadata Retention — Signal Knows Almost Nothing About Its Users
Signal's architecture is designed to minimise the metadata it retains about user communications. Signal does not store message content, contact lists, group memberships, profile photos, or the details of who communicates with whom. In legal proceedings where Signal has been compelled to produce user data, the only information available has been the account creation date and the date of last connection. This minimal metadata architecture means that even a legal investigation of Signal's systems would produce almost no information about a user's communication patterns — a standard that far exceeds any other mainstream messaging platform.
🔕
Screen Lock, Incognito Keyboard, and Screen Security Features
Signal includes a suite of additional privacy features beyond encryption and disappearing messages: a screen lock that requires a PIN or biometric authentication to open the app, an incognito keyboard option that prevents the device keyboard from learning typed words, and a screen security feature that prevents Signal from appearing in the device's recent apps overview — the screen stays blank when Signal is viewed in the app switcher. These features specifically address the concern of someone physically accessing the device and reviewing Signal content — providing layered protection against device-level review as well as network-level interception.
📝
Note to Self — Private Encrypted Storage on the Device
Signal includes a "Note to Self" feature — an encrypted private messaging thread with oneself that functions as a secure private notepad. Content stored in Note to Self is end-to-end encrypted and stored in Signal's encrypted database on the device. A child can use this feature as a private encrypted storage space for notes, images, or information they do not want accessible through the device's standard storage — functioning similarly to Aloha Browser's encrypted vault but within a mainstream messaging app that carries a gold-standard privacy reputation.

What Parents Should Know

1
Signal Is Genuinely Excellent — The Parental Concern Is Who Installed It and Why, Not What Signal Is
Signal deserves its outstanding reputation. It is developed by a non-profit organisation with a genuine commitment to privacy as a human right, is recommended by the most credible security researchers in the world, and has no advertising, no data collection, and no commercial incentive to compromise user privacy. A parent who restricts Signal should be clear with their child that the restriction is not because Signal is a bad or unsafe application — it is an excellent one. The restriction is about whether an application specifically designed to make communications invisible to everyone except the two parties is appropriate on a device that is the parent's legal and moral responsibility to oversee. That is a legitimate parental concern that has nothing to do with Signal being poorly designed or harmful.
2
A Child Who Has Signal Installed Has Typically Made a Deliberate and Informed Choice to Have It
Unlike many apps in this document that children adopt because peers use them or because they find them through search, Signal is not a casual social adoption — it is a deliberate choice. A child who has Signal installed has typically sought it out specifically, understands broadly what it does, and has chosen it because of its privacy properties. This deliberateness is important context for the conversation: a child who has Signal because they are interested in digital privacy and encryption as topics is presenting a very different situation from one who installed it the week after finding out their text messages are monitored. The timing and context of Signal's installation on the device — and the child's explanation for why they have it — are the most informative starting points for the conversation.
3
Disappearing Messages Mean There May Be No Content to Review — The Installation Itself Is the Primary Finding
Unlike Aloha Browser or Tellonym, where opening the app reveals content — a vault of stored media, a history of received anonymous messages — Signal with disappearing message timers active may reveal very little reviewable content even when opened. If message timers have been running, conversations may show empty or near-empty histories even if extensive communication has occurred. The existence of Signal on the device, and the presence of active conversations with disappearing message settings, is itself the primary finding — more informative than what is or is not visible in the message history. A parent who opens Signal and finds mostly empty conversation histories with disappearing message timers configured should not interpret this as evidence that little communication has occurred — it may indicate the opposite.
4
The Restriction Decision Involves a Genuine Tension Between Privacy and Oversight — Acknowledge That Tension With the Child
Signal presents the clearest case in this entire document of a genuine tension between two legitimate values: a child's developing right to privacy in their communications, and a parent's responsibility for their child's safety and wellbeing. Most platforms in this document are restricted because they carry specific harms — stranger contact, anonymous harassment, adult content, content filter bypass. Signal carries none of these specific harms. The restriction is based purely on the parental oversight gap its privacy design creates. A parent who restricts Signal and has a conversation with a child that acknowledges this genuine tension — that privacy in communication is a real and important value, but that the parent's responsibility for the child's safety means they need to be able to maintain some oversight of the child's communications at this stage of life — is more likely to have a productive conversation than one who treats Signal as obviously inappropriate without engaging with why the child values privacy.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps that reflect Signal's unique position — acknowledging its genuine quality, approaching the conversation around why the child has it rather than what it is, and making a restriction decision that honestly engages with the tension between privacy and oversight.

💬
Ask Why They Have Signal — and Engage Seriously With the Answer
The most important conversation about Signal is a simple question: why does the child have it? A child who says they installed Signal because they are interested in encryption and digital privacy — and who can describe what end-to-end encryption means, or who has encountered Signal through reading about online security — is presenting a genuine intellectual interest that deserves serious engagement. A child who says they got it because their friend told them their parents can't see Signal messages is presenting a different situation that deserves a different conversation. Both conversations should acknowledge that Signal is a genuinely excellent application — because it is, and a child who knows that will see through any conversation that treats it as otherwise. The conversation that lands most effectively with a privacy-aware teenager acknowledges that Signal is excellent, explains why parental oversight remains the parent's responsibility regardless of the quality of the tool used to circumvent it, and invites the child to consider whether the specific conversations they want to have on Signal are ones they would be comfortable discussing directly.
🔎
Open Signal and Note What Is Visible — Then Focus on Contact Identity and Disappearing Message Settings
If Signal is found on a child's device, open it and review what is visible. If disappearing message timers have been active on conversations, message content may not be visible — but the identities of contacts and the fact that disappearing timers are configured are visible and informative. Note: the contacts the child is communicating with, whether any contacts are unfamiliar names, whether disappearing message timers are configured on conversations and at what interval, and whether the Note to Self feature contains any content. Signal's screen lock feature means the parent may need to ask the child to unlock the app — this request itself is informative. A child who is reluctant to unlock Signal for a parent's review is providing meaningful information about what they expect would be found, even if the disappearing message settings have already removed the content.
🔒
Apply the iLimits Restriction If Warranted — and Consider Whether the Privacy Conversation Should Continue
Apply the iLimits restriction to prevent Signal from being installed or used on the child's device if the circumstances warrant it — particularly if the child's explanation for having Signal suggests circumvention of parental oversight rather than genuine privacy interest, or if the contact review reveals unfamiliar contacts with disappearing message settings configured. After applying the restriction, consider whether the broader privacy conversation is one worth continuing — because a child who is genuinely interested in digital privacy and encryption is a child whose interest is worth nurturing in age-appropriate ways. A parent who restricts Signal and then has a conversation about how encryption works, why privacy in communication is valuable for adults, and at what age and under what circumstances Signal or similar tools become appropriate, is doing something more durable and more educational than a restriction alone can achieve. The restriction closes the immediate oversight gap; the conversation gives the child a framework for thinking about privacy and parental responsibility that carries forward beyond the current decision.

Signal's designers built the most private consumer messaging application available — and they built it to be that way on purpose, because they believe private communication is a fundamental right. They are correct. A child, on a device a parent is responsible for, using an application specifically because it makes all communication invisible to the parent is a different situation — not because Signal is wrong, but because the child's age and the parent's responsibility make the oversight gap it creates a legitimate concern. iLimits restricts the app. The conversation explains the difference between privacy as a value and privacy as a tool for avoiding parental oversight — a distinction that matters now and that a child will carry forward.
