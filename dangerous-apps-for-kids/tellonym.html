What This App Does

A clear picture of Tellonym — an anonymous Q&A and messaging platform where any user can send a message or question to a named account holder without identifying themselves, the responses are posted publicly on the recipient's profile, and the account holder has no way to identify or block the anonymous senders of harmful messages before those messages have been received.

💬
Tellonym Lets Any Anonymous User Send Messages Directly to a Named, Identified Child — Creating an Unblockable Channel for Harassment, Bullying, and Grooming
Tellonym is an anonymous Q&A platform that allows any user — whether registered or not — to send messages or questions to any account holder without revealing their identity. The account holder's profile is public and identified — unlike platforms where both parties are anonymous, Tellonym creates a specific asymmetry: the recipient is a named, identifiable person, while every sender is completely anonymous. This asymmetry means that a child who creates a Tellonym account is immediately accessible to anonymous messages from anyone who knows their username — including people from their school, community, or from adult strangers — without any ability to screen, filter, or identify who is contacting them before the message has been received.

🎯
Asymmetric Anonymity — The Sender Is Anonymous, the Recipient Is Named
Unlike platforms where all users are anonymous to each other, Tellonym creates a structural asymmetry: the account holder — typically a child who has shared their Tellonym link with their peer group — is publicly identified by their real name or username, while every person who sends them a message is completely anonymous. This means the child is publicly exposed and identified while everyone who contacts them is completely protected. An abusive or harmful message cannot be traced back to its sender, cannot be responded to privately, and can be sent by anyone who has the child's Tellonym link regardless of whether they have an account on the platform.
😔
A Primary Vehicle for Anonymous Cyberbullying and Targeted Harassment
The combination of complete sender anonymity and named, identified recipients makes Tellonym structurally ideal for anonymous harassment. A classmate, a peer, or anyone who knows the child's Tellonym link can send cruel, humiliating, or abusive messages without any risk of identification or consequence. The recipient receives the message — including all of its emotional impact — without any ability to identify who sent it, report them by identity, or prevent further messages from the same source. Tellonym and similar anonymous Q&A platforms have been specifically associated with cyberbullying incidents and, in documented cases, with messages that have contributed to serious harm to the mental health and wellbeing of young recipients.
👤
No Verification for Senders — Adults Can Contact Children Without Any Account
Anyone can send a message to a Tellonym account holder without registering an account, without providing any identity, and without any age verification. An adult who wishes to contact a child whose Tellonym link is publicly available needs nothing more than the link — no account, no identity, no verification. The child cannot determine whether an anonymous message has come from a classmate or from an adult they have never met. The platform provides no mechanism for screening senders by age, identity, or account status before a message is received.
📣
Responses Are Posted Publicly on the Profile — Creating Public Humiliation Dynamics
When a Tellonym account holder responds to an anonymous message, both the message and the response are posted publicly on their profile. This creates a social dynamic where an anonymous sender can post a message designed to embarrass, humiliate, or manipulate the recipient into a public response — and the recipient either responds publicly, potentially amplifying the embarrassing content, or declines to respond, leaving the message visible on their profile without context. The public nature of the message-response format creates dynamics that can be exploited for public humiliation alongside direct private harm.
🔗
Links Are Shared on Other Social Platforms — Expanding the Pool of Anonymous Senders
Children typically share their Tellonym profile link on their other social media accounts — Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat — inviting their followers to send them anonymous messages. This sharing extends the pool of potential anonymous senders from the Tellonym user base to the much larger audience of every follower the child has on other platforms, as well as anyone those followers share the link with. A child's Tellonym link can reach people they do not know, people they have blocked on other platforms, and people from entirely different social contexts — all of whom gain anonymous messaging access to the child through the shared link.

👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
Children Create Tellonym Accounts Because Peer Groups Use Them — They May Not Understand the Risk Until They Receive a Harmful Message
The primary motivation for creating a Tellonym account is social — a peer group uses the platform, and a child creates an account to participate in the same social dynamic, often sharing their link on their social media profiles because their friends have done so. The child may understand that they will receive anonymous messages, but typically has not thought through what happens when an anonymous message is cruel, humiliating, or designed to hurt. The risk often only becomes apparent after the first harmful message is received — at which point the child has already shared their link widely, anonymous senders already have access to their profile, and there is no way to retract the messages already received. Prevention — before the account is created and the link is shared — is the only point at which the risk can be fully avoided.
2
The Mental Health Impact of Anonymous Harassment Is Distinct and Serious
Research on cyberbullying consistently identifies anonymous harassment as particularly harmful compared to identified bullying — in part because the anonymity removes the child's ability to attribute the message to a specific person and understand why it was sent, and in part because the child cannot know whether the message represents one person or many. A cruel anonymous message can feel like it comes from an unknown number of unknown people — it cannot be contextualised as coming from a specific person with a specific grievance — which makes it more destabilising and more difficult to process than a harmful message from an identified source. Parents should take anonymous harassment through platforms like Tellonym seriously as a mental health concern, not only as a safety concern. If a child has received harmful anonymous messages through Tellonym, the conversation should address both how they are feeling about what was sent and the steps being taken to prevent further contact.
3
Sharing the Link on Other Platforms Has Already Extended Anonymous Access — Deleting the App Does Not Retract the Link
A child who has created a Tellonym account and shared the link on their Instagram, TikTok, or Snapchat profile has already given anonymous messaging access to everyone who has seen that link — including people who may have saved or reshared it. Deleting the Tellonym app from the device does not remove the child's profile from the platform, does not retract the link from anywhere it has been shared, and does not prevent people who already have the link from sending anonymous messages to the profile. A complete response to finding Tellonym on a child's device includes deleting the Tellonym account itself — not just the app — and removing the Tellonym link from any social media profiles where it has been shared. Both steps are necessary to fully close the anonymous messaging access that was opened when the child created their account.
4
A Child Who Has Received Harmful Anonymous Messages May Not Disclose This Without a Direct and Gentle Conversation
Children who have received cruel or humiliating anonymous messages on Tellonym often do not disclose this to a parent — either because they are embarrassed by the content of what was sent, because they do not want to appear unable to handle peer social dynamics, because they are not sure a parent will understand the context, or because they are trying to manage the emotional impact themselves. A parent who discovers Tellonym on a child's device and opens the conversation should begin by looking at the messages that have already been received — visible within the app — and approach the conversation from a position of supporting the child rather than interrogating them. If harmful messages have been received, the most important thing the child needs to know is that the content of the message says something about the person who sent it and nothing about their worth — and that bringing this to a parent is the right response, not a sign of weakness.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps that reflect Tellonym's specific harm mechanism — checking messages before deleting, ensuring the account and link are fully removed rather than just the app, and approaching any conversation about received harmful messages with the child's emotional wellbeing at the centre.

💬
Approach the Conversation With the Child's Emotional Wellbeing First — Especially If Harmful Messages Have Been Received
If Tellonym is found on a child's device and the message history shows harmful content, the most important first step is checking how the child is feeling about what they have received — before addressing the platform, the restriction, or anything else. A child who has been receiving cruel or humiliating anonymous messages may have been managing significant distress without disclosure, and the conversation that surfaces this needs to prioritise the child's emotional state before the safety discussion. Make clear that the content of anonymous messages reflects only on the person who sent them and says nothing true about the child who received them. Ensure the child knows that bringing this kind of thing to a parent is exactly the right response — and that the parental response will be supportive rather than focused on blame or restriction before understanding. The restriction of the platform is a step that follows from ensuring the child is supported; it should not be the opening move in the conversation.
🔎
Review Messages Within the App, Then Delete the Account — Not Just the App
Open Tellonym on the child's device and review the anonymous messages that have been received before taking any further steps — these may reveal whether the child has been experiencing harassment, and the content may be relevant context for the conversation. After reviewing, delete the Tellonym account itself through the account settings within the app — not just the app from the device. Deleting only the app leaves the profile active, the link valid, and anonymous messages continuing to be receivable if someone sends to the link. The account deletion removes the profile and deactivates the link. After deleting the account, check the child's other social media profiles — Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat — for any Tellonym link that has been posted in their bio, stories, or posts, and remove those links to prevent anyone who saves them from being able to send messages if the account were ever reinstated.
🔒
Block Tellonym Through iLimits and Discuss Anonymous Q&A Platforms as a Category
Apply the iLimits restriction to block Tellonym on the child's device, and extend the conversation to include anonymous Q&A platforms as a category rather than addressing only this specific app. Tellonym is one of several anonymous Q&A platforms — NGL (Not Gonna Lie), ASKfm, and similar apps operate on the same asymmetric anonymity model. A child who is blocked from Tellonym may find an alternative if the underlying social dynamic — peer groups using anonymous Q&A apps — continues. A broader conversation about why the asymmetric anonymity structure of these platforms makes them specifically risky — the sender is always protected, the recipient is always exposed, and harmful messages cannot be prevented before they are received — gives the child a framework for evaluating similar platforms independently, rather than simply knowing that one specific app has been restricted.
