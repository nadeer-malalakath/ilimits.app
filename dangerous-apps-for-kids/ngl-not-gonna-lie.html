📱 Section 1

What This App Does

A clear picture of NGL — an anonymous messaging app linked to Instagram that allows anyone to send a child anonymous messages through a link shared on their Instagram story, with no accountability for senders and a business model that uses the desire to know who sent a message to drive paid subscriptions.

💬
NGL Lets Anyone Send Your Child an Anonymous Message Through Their Instagram Story — With No Way to Know Who It Came From
NGL (Not Gonna Lie) is an anonymous Q&A and messaging app that integrates with Instagram. A child shares an NGL link on their Instagram story; anyone who sees the story — followers, or anyone who sees a reshare — can send an anonymous message to that child. The child receives the messages in the NGL app but does not know who sent them. There is no accountability mechanism for senders, and the combination of a real named child's Instagram account with a fully anonymous inbound message channel creates a direct route for anonymous bullying, harassment and harmful content targeted at a specific individual.
📸
NGL Links Directly to Instagram — Strangers, Acquaintances and Peers Can All Send Anonymous Messages Through a Child's Story
Because NGL operates through an Instagram story link, the pool of potential anonymous senders is not just a child's close friends — it is everyone who can see their Instagram story. Depending on account privacy settings, this may include strangers, distant acquaintances, older students, and anyone the child follows or who follows them. A private Instagram account limits this pool somewhat, but does not restrict it to people the child genuinely trusts. The anonymous message channel is as open as the child's Instagram story visibility.

💬
Anyone Can Send a Child an Anonymous Message Through Their Instagram Story
NGL works by generating a link that a child shares as a sticker on their Instagram story. Anyone who sees the story can tap the link and send an anonymous message — which the child receives in the NGL app alongside their other anonymous messages. The sender's identity is completely hidden. A child who uses NGL has opened a fully anonymous inbound message channel connected to their real named Instagram account, accessible to everyone who can see their story.
🎭
No Accountability for Senders — Anonymous Harassment Has No Consequence
NGL's fundamental design provides no accountability to senders. A person who sends a cruel, threatening or harassing message through NGL faces no social consequence — their identity is never revealed to the recipient. This creates the same dynamic as other anonymous platforms: the anonymity lowers the inhibition threshold for sending harmful messages, and the inability to identify the sender means a child who receives harassment cannot report a specific person or know which member of their social network sent it.
💰
Paid Subscription Exploits the Desire to Know Who Sent a Message
NGL's revenue model is built around the desire to know who sent an anonymous message. The app offers "hints" about sender identity to paid subscribers — with the strong implication that paying will reveal who sent a message. In practice, these hints are vague and often uninformative, and full identity reveal is not offered. The model specifically exploits the psychological pull of wanting to know who said something about you, monetising a child's curiosity (and potential distress) by creating a recurring incentive to purchase subscriptions. This is a commercial practice that exploits the specific vulnerability the anonymous messaging format creates.
📢
Messages Can Be Reshared to Instagram Stories — Amplifying Content About the Child
NGL allows children to reshare received anonymous messages back to their Instagram story — displaying the anonymous message (and their response) to their entire follower base. This resharing feature is designed to drive engagement and encourage more people to send messages, but it also creates a mechanism by which anonymous messages — including harmful or embarrassing ones — can be amplified through a child's own social profile, potentially reaching a wider audience than the original anonymous sender intended or the child realised.
👥
Specifically Targeted at the Individual — Unlike Broadcast Anonymous Platforms
Unlike Yik Yak, which creates a shared anonymous community feed, NGL messages are directed specifically at a named individual. Every message a child receives on NGL was sent by someone who saw their Instagram story, tapped their link, and chose to send them specifically a message. This specificity means that any harassment or harmful content received is deliberately and personally targeted at the child — not incidentally encountered while browsing a feed. The personal targeting makes harmful messages more psychologically impactful than content encountered in a general anonymous forum.

💡
Children Use NGL Because They Want Validation — The App Exploits This Healthy Need in a Way That Reliably Produces Harm
The appeal of NGL is not hard to understand: it promises anonymous feedback from your social circle — potentially including kind messages, compliments, or confirmation that people think well of you. This is a completely normal and healthy desire. The problem is that anonymous feedback from peers is as likely to be unkind as kind, that the format specifically lowers inhibition for sending cruel messages, and that the inability to identify the sender means a child cannot distinguish between feedback from a close friend and feedback from someone who dislikes them. Understanding why a child was using NGL — the desire for validation, not malicious intent — is the most useful starting point for the conversation about why the app is restricted.
👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
A Child Using NGL Was Looking for Validation — Not Deliberately Seeking Out Risk
The reason children use NGL — and the entire genre of anonymous Q&A apps it belongs to — is that they want to know what people think of them. This is a normal and understandable desire, particularly in adolescence when identity and social standing feel especially important. A child who installed NGL was not making a reckless choice; they were responding to a product that specifically markets itself as a way to get honest, anonymous feedback from the people in your social life. Understanding this motivation — rather than treating the app's presence as evidence of poor judgement — makes the conversation about why NGL is restricted more productive. The conversation that acknowledges the legitimate underlying desire while explaining the specific harm the anonymous format creates is more likely to be heard than a prohibition without context.
2
If Your Child Has Received Harmful Messages Through NGL, Treat It With the Same Seriousness as Other Forms of Targeted Harassment
Anonymous messages received through NGL are personally targeted at a specific child by someone who deliberately chose to send them. The fact that they were anonymous does not make them less real or less harmful than named harassment — and in some respects the anonymity makes them more difficult to process, because the child cannot know which person in their social circle sent them. If your child has received content that constitutes bullying, harassment or a threat through NGL, take it seriously: preserve screenshots, report through the app, and if relevant involve school administration or law enforcement in the same way you would for any other targeted harassment. The anonymity of the sender does not reduce the severity of the content or the child's need for support.
3
The "Hints" Feature Is a Commercial Practice That Exploits a Child's Distress — Not a Real Tool for Identifying Senders
NGL's paid subscription offers "hints" about who sent anonymous messages, implying that paying will reveal sender identity. These hints are typically vague — location data, device type, or broad demographic information — and do not reliably identify a specific person. A child who has received upsetting messages and is paying for NGL hints to find out who sent them is in a particularly harmful situation: they are spending money on a feature that is unlikely to resolve their distress, while continuing to engage with the app and potentially receive more harmful messages. If you discover your child has subscribed to NGL for this reason, the priority is addressing the distress about the messages themselves — not the sender-identification quest — and removing the financial incentive to remain engaged with the platform.
4
NGL Is Part of a Category — Similar Apps Have Repeatedly Caused the Same Harm
NGL is the current prominent example of an anonymous Q&A format that has cycled through multiple apps — Ask.fm, Sarahah, Tellonym, and others before it — each of which has followed the same pattern: viral adoption by teenagers seeking validation, significant documented harm through anonymous harassment, eventual decline, and replacement by the next equivalent app. The specific platform changes; the format and the harm remain consistent. Restriction at the category level — anonymous messaging and Q&A apps — is more durable than blocking individual apps as they rise and fall, because the category will continue to produce new variants as long as the format exists and teenagers seek validation from their social circles.

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps for NGL — including how to have the most productive conversation about the validation appeal, how to respond if harmful messages have been received, and why category-level blocking is the most durable approach for anonymous Q&A apps.

💬
Start With the Underlying Desire — Then Explain Why the Anonymous Format Reliably Produces Harm
The most productive conversation about NGL begins by acknowledging what the app was trying to provide: the chance to hear what people honestly think of you, without the awkwardness of asking directly. This is a real and understandable desire. Then explain why the anonymous format specifically makes this unreliable and harmful: without any consequence for sending unkind messages, the feedback a child receives through NGL is systematically skewed toward whatever someone feels comfortable saying when there is no accountability. A kind friend who thinks well of your child may or may not send a message; someone who dislikes them has no barrier to expressing that. The format reliably produces a mix that is harder to interpret and more likely to include harmful content than any feedback the child would receive in a normal social interaction. Helping your child understand this dynamic — rather than simply removing the app — gives them a framework for evaluating the entire category of anonymous feedback apps as they encounter them in the future.
🔍
If Harmful Messages Were Received, Address the Distress First — Not the Platform
If your child has received upsetting anonymous messages through NGL, the first priority is their emotional state — not the restriction or the conversation about the app's design. Ask how they are feeling about the messages, whether the content was surprising or confirmed fears they already had, and whether they have a sense of who might have sent harmful content. Validate the distress directly: receiving an unkind anonymous message from someone in your social circle is genuinely unpleasant, and the inability to know who sent it makes it harder, not easier, to process. Make clear they did not deserve the messages and that the sender's decision to be anonymous while being unkind says something about the sender, not about them. After addressing the emotional dimension, discuss reporting the specific messages through the app and, if the content constitutes a genuine threat, the possibility of involving a trusted adult at school or, in serious cases, law enforcement.
🔒
Block NGL at the Category Level — Anonymous Q&A Apps Follow a Predictable Cycle
Apply iLimits category-level restrictions to block NGL and the broader anonymous Q&A messaging category — this covers NGL alongside past and future equivalents in a single restriction and remains effective as the category produces new apps. Enable Family Link's app installation approval requirement to generate an alert if a similar app is subsequently installed. Also review your child's Instagram privacy settings: a public or semi-public Instagram account gives NGL's anonymous link access to a significantly broader pool of senders than a strictly private account. Tightening Instagram privacy settings reduces the pool of people who can send anonymous messages even if a similar app is used in future — and is a useful step independent of the NGL restriction. The combination of category-level app restriction, Family Link approval, and reviewed Instagram privacy settings gives the most complete coverage of the anonymous messaging concern.

Wanting to Know What People Think of You Is Normal — Anonymous Messages Are Not a Safe Way to Find Out

NGL exists because teenagers want to know what their peers think of them — a legitimate and entirely human desire. The anonymous format does not deliver honest peer feedback; it delivers whatever people feel comfortable saying when there is no consequence for saying it. The result is a channel that is reliably more harmful than helpful and that commercially exploits the distress it creates. iLimits gives you the tools to block it. The conversation that acknowledges what your child was actually looking for — and explains why anonymous feedback is not the same as honest feedback — gives them something more useful than a restriction alone.
