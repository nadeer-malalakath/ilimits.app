Thunder VPN creates an encrypted tunnel between the device and its servers, routing all internet traffic through that tunnel. Every network-level parental control — DNS content filters, router category blocks, household monitoring systems — is bypassed for any session in which Thunder VPN is connected. Thunder VPN encrypts the traffic it carries, meaning network monitoring tools that log website visits or app connections see only a connection to Thunder VPN's servers during active sessions. This removal of monitoring visibility applies to all encrypted VPN services and is a structural feature of the technology, not specific to Thunder VPN's implementation.

Minimal Developer Transparency — Obscure Corporate Identity
The developer identity behind Thunder VPN has limited public information associated with it. Unlike NordVPN (Nord Security), ProtonVPN (Proton AG) or TunnelBear (owned by McAfee), Thunder VPN does not have a clearly identified, publicly accountable corporate entity behind it. A child whose traffic is routed through Thunder VPN is sending their browsing data to servers operated by an entity whose identity, jurisdiction and data handling practices are not meaningfully verifiable by a consumer. This opacity is a meaningful differentiator from more transparent providers in the same category.

Free With No Clear Premium Revenue Model — Raises Data Practice Questions
Thunder VPN is free to use with no obvious premium tier or clearly stated revenue model. Operating a VPN infrastructure — servers across multiple countries, bandwidth, maintenance — has significant costs that a free product must cover through some means. Without transparency about how the service is funded and what data practices generate that revenue, the question of what happens to a child's browsing traffic while it is routed through Thunder VPN's servers cannot be answered with any confidence. This uncertainty is the same concern that applies to VPN Master and other opaque free VPN products.

👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
Thunder VPN Is in the Lower-Transparency Tier of Consumer VPN Products
The consumer VPN market spans a wide range of provider quality. At one end are services like ProtonVPN and NordVPN — with published audits, verified no-logs policies and clearly identified corporate entities. At the other end are services like Thunder VPN, VPN Master and similar products — with obscure developer identities, no published audits and unclear revenue models. Thunder VPN sits firmly in the lower-transparency tier. For child safety purposes, both ends of this spectrum create a network bypass concern, but the lower-transparency tier adds a data practice concern on top. Parents who are inclined to treat the bypass risk as the sole concern should still factor in the opacity of the provider when evaluating what is actually happening to their child's traffic.
2
The "Free" Model for VPN Services Is Not Neutral — Someone Is Covering the Costs
Providing a VPN service requires real infrastructure — servers in multiple countries, bandwidth capacity, security maintenance and support. These costs are significant and must be covered somehow. For premium services like NordVPN, the revenue model is clear: subscription fees. For free services like Thunder VPN, where there is no obvious premium tier or advertising to account for the infrastructure costs, the question of what funds the service is a reasonable one to ask. It may be advertising revenue from limited in-app ads, data analytics, traffic monetisation, or other practices — the point is that it is not disclosed and not independently verified. Children routing their traffic through a service with an unclear revenue model are accepting a level of uncertainty about how their data is handled that they would not accept from a paid, audited alternative.
3
Thunder VPN's High Download Count Does Not Indicate Safety or Trustworthiness
A very high download count on Google Play indicates that a product is widely used — it does not indicate that the product is safe, well-run or trustworthy from a data practice perspective. Some of the most downloaded VPN products on the Play Store are among the least transparent about their operations. Children who encounter Thunder VPN through an app store search may perceive the high download count as a positive signal — a reasonable heuristic for many app categories, but not a reliable one for VPN tools where download volume and corporate transparency are entirely unrelated. This is worth addressing directly in any conversation about why Thunder VPN is restricted rather than simply accepted because it has many users.
4
Category-Level Blocking Is Especially Important for Tools Like Thunder VPN
Thunder VPN belongs to a large category of similar products — free, high-download, low-transparency consumer VPN apps — where new entrants appear regularly and existing products are frequently rebranded. Blocking Thunder VPN individually without addressing the broader category leaves a child able to switch to any of a large number of functionally identical alternatives that may be even less transparent. Category-level blocking through iLimits addresses this class of product comprehensively rather than requiring each new entrant to be individually identified and blocked. For the lower-transparency end of the VPN market, category-level restriction is not just more convenient — it is the only approach that provides durable coverage.

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps for Thunder VPN — including how to address the "free and popular" perception directly, why category-level blocking is the most durable approach, and how to layer device and network controls effectively.

💬
Address the "Free and Popular Means Safe" Assumption Directly
A child who has installed Thunder VPN may be drawing on the reasonable heuristic that a product with tens of millions of downloads is probably trustworthy. This heuristic works well for many app categories but breaks down entirely for free VPN tools. The most useful conversation addresses this directly: explain that in the VPN category, download count tells you nothing about whether a service handles user data responsibly. More than that, a product that operates for free with no clear revenue model and no public accountability for how it funds itself is one where the question "what are they doing with the traffic" cannot be answered. For a product where all browsing traffic passes through the company's servers, that unanswered question matters. Contrast this with what transparency actually looks like in the VPN market — published audit results, a named corporate entity, a clear privacy policy with independent verification — and help your child understand the difference between marketed credibility (high download count) and substantiated credibility (audit results, known corporate identity).
🗂️
Apply Category-Level VPN Blocking — Thunder VPN Is One of Many Equivalent Alternatives
Thunder VPN occupies a large and competitive category — free, high-download, low-transparency consumer VPN apps — where new products appear regularly and existing products are frequently rebranded or replaced. Blocking Thunder VPN individually without addressing the category leaves a child able to switch to any of a large number of functionally identical alternatives within an app store search. Apply iLimits restrictions at the VPN and proxy category level to cover the entire class of products. Category-level blocking is particularly important for this end of the market because it provides coverage of tools that have not yet been individually identified, are rebranded versions of known products, or emerge as new alternatives after a specific restriction has been put in place.
