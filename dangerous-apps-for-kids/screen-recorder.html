📱 Section 1

What This App Does

A clear picture of Screen Recorder — a tool that captures everything displayed on a device's screen as video footage, including app content, video calls, messages and any on-screen activity, with the ability to save, share or upload the resulting recordings.

⏺️
Screen Recorder Captures Everything on the Device Screen — Including Video Calls, Private Messages and App Content Designed Not to Be Recorded
Screen Recorder apps capture the device's full screen output as video — recording everything visible during active use, including private video calls, messages, photos, and app content whose publishers specifically prevent screenshot or recording through platform controls. The resulting video can be saved to the device, shared through any platform, or uploaded anywhere. On a child's device, the combination of covert recording capability, the circumvention of recording protections in apps like Snapchat and Signal, and the potential for recorded content to be shared or weaponised is the primary concern.
⚖️
Screen Recorders Have Legitimate Uses — The Concern Is the Combination of Covert Capability and a Child's Specific Online Vulnerabilities
Screen recording is a legitimate tool for device tutorials, gaming content, bug reports and many professional uses. The concern on a child's device is specifically about the combination of features most used in harmful contexts: silent or background recording of video calls, capturing content from apps that block screenshots, and the potential for recorded intimate or private material to be shared without the consent of the person in it. This makes restriction the appropriate default on children's devices, particularly for adolescents.

Unlike a screenshot, which captures a single frame, a screen recording captures live video including real-time conversations, video calls, faces, and audio, creating a comprehensive record that can be used, shared, or uploaded anywhere the user chooses.

Can Record Video Calls Including the Other Person's Image and Voice
Screen recording during a video call captures not only the child using the device but also the person on the other end — their face, voice, and anything visible in their environment. The person being recorded typically has no way of knowing a recording is taking place, as screen recording apps do not notify other call participants. This creates a direct route to recording a person's image and voice without their knowledge or consent, with the recording immediately available for sharing through any platform.
🔓
Bypasses Screenshot and Recording Protections in Apps Like Snapchat and Signal
Many apps — including Snapchat, Signal, banking apps, and streaming services — implement screenshot and screen recording prevention at the software level, blocking the device's native screenshot and recording functions. Third-party screen recording apps bypass these protections entirely by operating at a lower level of the system or through accessibility APIs. A child using a screen recorder can capture content from Snapchat, Signal or any other app that believes its content is protected from recording — including disappearing messages, photos shared with the expectation they would not be saved, and private conversations.
🔇
Can Operate Silently in the Background Without Obvious Visual Indicators
Many screen recording apps can be minimised or run from a persistent notification while appearing to the casual observer to be inactive. A recording that was started before a call or conversation begins and minimised during it will capture the entire interaction without any visible recording interface on screen. The person on the other end of a call has no indication that a recording is taking place, and other people who might glance at the device's screen during a recording session may not notice a small persistent indicator in the status bar.
📤
Recordings Are Immediately Available for Sharing Through Any Platform
Once a screen recording is saved — which happens automatically when the recording is stopped — the resulting video is stored in the device's standard video library alongside other photos and videos. It can be shared through any messaging app, social platform, or file transfer method available on the device, and can be uploaded to any online service. There is no delay, no additional confirmation step, and no record within the screen recorder app of where the recording was subsequently shared.

Why It May Be Risky for Children

Screen Recorder's risks for children centre on image-based abuse and non-consensual recording — capturing others without consent during calls, bypassing recording protections in messaging apps, and creating material that can be shared, weaponised or used for coercion.

🎭
Non-Consensual Recording of Video Calls
Screen recording during video calls captures the other person's face, voice and environment without their knowledge — creating image and audio recordings of individuals who have not consented to being recorded and have no way of knowing it is happening
🔓
Bypasses Snapchat, Signal and Other App Protections
Third-party screen recorders bypass the screenshot and recording prevention built into apps like Snapchat and Signal — enabling capture of disappearing messages, protected photos and private conversations that the sender intended to be temporary or unrecordable
😰
Image-Based Abuse and Content Without Consent
Recordings of video calls involving intimate content, or recordings of protected app content showing private images, can be used for coercion, threats, or non-consensual distribution — a serious harm specifically associated with adolescent use of screen recording tools
🔇
Silent Background Recording Possible
Screen recorder apps can minimise to a status bar notification and continue recording in the background — making covert recording of interactions on a shared device, or recording of a call without the other participant's awareness, straightforward to achieve
📤
Immediate Sharability — No Barrier to Distribution
Completed recordings save instantly to the device's video library and are immediately shareable through any platform — there is no friction between capturing content without consent and distributing it to any audience
👥
Peer Pressure and Social Coercion Contexts
Adolescents may be pressured by peers to use screen recorders to capture private conversations or calls, or may not fully understand the implications of recording someone else's image without their knowledge in the context of a relationship or friendship
🟡
Screen Recorder Carries a Medium–High Risk Rating Screen recording tools have legitimate adult uses, but the combination of covert recording capability, bypass of app recording protections, potential for non-consensual content capture, and immediate sharability creates specific and serious risks for children and teenagers that warrant restriction as the default on their devices.
⚖️
Recording Someone Without Their Consent Raises Serious Legal and Ethical Issues — Children May Not Fully Understand the Implications
Recording a person's image, voice or private conversations without their knowledge and consent raises legal concerns in most jurisdictions — many countries and states have laws specifically addressing non-consensual recording of video calls and private conversations. Children and teenagers may not be aware that recording a friend's call or capturing content from a private message exchange without consent has serious ethical and potentially legal implications, particularly where the recorded content is subsequently shared. This context is worth addressing directly in any conversation about screen recording tools.
👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
Screen Recording Is Significantly Different From Taking a Screenshot — It Creates Video of Real People Without Their Consent
Most children and teenagers understand that taking a screenshot of a private message is generally considered rude or a breach of trust. The same social awareness does not always extend to screen recording — which does significantly more. A screen recording of a video call creates a video that captures the other person's face, voice, mannerisms and environment in real time, for as long as the recording runs, without any indication to the person being recorded that a video is being made of them. This is a qualitatively different kind of content from a screenshot, with different implications for how it can be used and shared. Making this distinction explicitly in conversation is more useful than treating screen recording as simply "a better screenshot."
2
The Concern Is Not Only the Child Doing the Recording — They May Also Be the Subject of Recording
The risk from screen recording tools on a child's device runs in both directions. A child may use a screen recorder to record others — creating the non-consensual recording risks described above. But a child may also be the subject of recording by someone else using a screen recorder on their device. A peer who starts a video call and records it without the child's knowledge — capturing the child's face, location, or any content the child shares during the call — is doing exactly what this page describes. Parents should address both dimensions: the importance of not recording others without consent, and the awareness that other people may use the same tools to record their child.
3
Adolescents in Particular Are Vulnerable to the Image-Based Abuse Pathway Screen Recording Enables
The specific harm pathway that makes screen recording a serious concern for adolescents is image-based abuse: the coercive use of intimate or embarrassing recordings to threaten, control or harm the person in them. A teenager who shares intimate content during a video call they believe to be private may not consider that the other person could be recording it — and screen recording apps make that recording completely invisible to the person being recorded. The recorded content can then be used for coercion or non-consensual distribution. This is not a hypothetical scenario — it is a documented and increasingly common harm pathway involving adolescent relationships. Discussing it directly, as a real risk rather than a theoretical one, is more effective than a vague warning about online safety.
4
Restricting Screen Recorder Apps Does Not Prevent All Screen Recording — But It Removes the Most Accessible Tool
Some Android devices have a built-in screen recording feature in their system settings or quick-settings panel. Restricting third-party screen recorder apps through iLimits removes the most accessible and feature-rich tools — including those specifically designed to minimise visibility, bypass app protections, and operate silently in the background — but does not necessarily prevent use of any native system-level screen recording capability the device itself provides. Parents should be aware of this distinction and, where appropriate, review device system settings to understand what recording capabilities are built in and whether they can be separately managed through device-level controls or Family Link settings.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps for addressing screen recording tools — including the consent and image-based abuse conversation, awareness of device-level recording capabilities, and how to address the risk in both directions.

💬
Have a Direct Conversation About Consent, Recording and Image-Based Abuse
The most important conversation about screen recording is not primarily about the technical capability — it is about what recording someone without their knowledge or consent actually means in the context of relationships and trust. Explain clearly and directly: recording a video call without telling the other person is not just rude — in many places it is illegal, and when the recording involves intimate content it can cause serious and lasting harm to the person recorded. Address the image-based abuse pathway directly with adolescents: if someone records you during a call without your knowledge and uses that recording to threaten or pressure you, that is a serious harm — and if you record someone else in that way, you are doing the same thing to them. This conversation is most effective when it is calm, specific and treats the child as capable of understanding the implications. Also discuss the reverse: helping your child understand that others may use screen recording tools against them, what to watch for, and that they can come to you if they believe they have been recorded without consent.
⏺️
Block Third-Party Screen Recorder Apps and Review Device-Level Recording Capabilities
Apply iLimits restrictions to block third-party screen recording apps across all of your child's devices. This removes the most feature-rich and covert recording tools — those specifically designed to minimise visibility, bypass app protections, and operate silently. After applying the restriction, review the device's own built-in screen recording capability: on many Android devices, a screen recording shortcut exists in the quick-settings panel (the dropdown from the top of the screen). If this built-in capability is a concern, check whether it can be managed through Family Link's content restrictions or device management settings. The combination of blocking third-party apps and reviewing native capabilities gives the most complete coverage.
🔒
Encourage Your Child to Use Apps With Strong Recording Protections and to Understand Their Own Privacy
For messaging and video call apps where your child communicates privately, encourage the use of platforms that implement recording notification features — some calling apps notify participants when a recording starts, providing transparency that screen recorder apps remove. Help your child understand their own privacy rights in digital communication: they have the right to expect that private calls and conversations are not being recorded without their knowledge. Encourage them to be thoughtful about what they share in video calls and private messages, not from a position of fear, but from an understanding that digital content can persist in ways that spoken conversations do not. If your child tells you they believe they have been recorded without consent, take it seriously and help them understand what steps they can take — including reporting to the platform involved and, in serious cases, to appropriate authorities.
