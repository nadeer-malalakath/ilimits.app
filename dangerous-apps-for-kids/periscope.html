📱 Section 1

What This Platform Does

A clear picture of Periscope — Twitter's live video streaming platform that allows users to broadcast in real time to a public or private audience, with live commenting, viewer location data, and streams that can be replayed after broadcast ends.

📡
Periscope Allows Anyone to Broadcast Live Video to the Public — With Real-Time Comments From Unknown Viewers and No Broadcast Delay
Periscope is a live video streaming platform originally developed as a standalone app and subsequently integrated into Twitter, allowing users to broadcast live video to anyone who chooses to watch — publicly, without prior approval of viewers. Broadcasts are accompanied by a live comment stream visible to both the broadcaster and all viewers, allowing unknown members of the public to send messages directly to a child who is live on camera in real time. There is no broadcast delay that would allow inappropriate content to be intercepted before reaching viewers, no meaningful age verification for broadcasters or viewers, and no parental control features of any kind. Although Periscope as a standalone app was officially shut down in 2021, its live streaming functionality continues through Twitter (now X), and the platform name and its associated risks remain relevant to parents whose children use the Twitter ecosystem.

Twitter Integration
Periscope is a live video broadcasting platform that allows users to go live in front of a public or restricted audience, receive real-time comments and reactions from viewers, share their location at the time of broadcast, and have their streams saved for replay by anyone who missed them live. The platform requires no age verification to broadcast or view content, and has no parental control features.
📹
Real-Time Public Video Broadcasting to an Unknown Audience
Periscope's core feature is the ability to broadcast live video to the public — meaning that when a child goes live, their face, voice, home environment and anything visible in frame can be seen by any member of the public who finds or is notified of the stream. Public streams are discoverable by all users of the platform, not only followers or contacts. A child who starts a public broadcast is effectively appearing live on camera in front of an audience that could include adults with no legitimate relationship to them, with no ability to screen who is watching before the broadcast begins.
💬
Live Comment Stream Visible to Broadcaster in Real Time
During a live broadcast, viewers can send comments that appear on the broadcaster's screen in real time — meaning a child who is live on camera receives messages from unknown members of the public while actively broadcasting. There is no moderation delay between a comment being sent and it appearing on the broadcaster's screen. Viewers can send inappropriate, sexual, threatening or manipulative messages to a child who is live on camera, with no buffer or filtering between sender and recipient during the broadcast.
📍
Optional Location Sharing That Can Reveal a Broadcaster's Position
Periscope includes the option to share a broadcaster's location at the time of a stream, which can reveal where a child is broadcasting from — including, in many cases, their home address or the address of their school. While location sharing is optional rather than mandatory, children may not understand the implications of enabling this feature or may be encouraged by viewers to share their location during a broadcast. Even without explicit location sharing, environmental details visible in a broadcast can allow determined viewers to identify a child's location.
🔁
Broadcasts Are Saved and Replayable After the Stream Ends
Periscope streams are saved and can be replayed by viewers after the live broadcast ends, meaning that content broadcast by a child does not disappear when the stream is over. A child who broadcasts their face, home environment, school uniform or any other identifying information creates a permanent record that can be viewed, saved or shared by anyone who accesses the replay. The child may have no awareness of who has saved or distributed their broadcast content after it ends.
🐦
Integration With Twitter Extends Audience Reach Significantly
Periscope's integration with Twitter — and its continuation as a live streaming feature within the Twitter/X platform — means that a live broadcast can be shared automatically to a user's Twitter followers and, in the case of public accounts, to the entire Twitter audience. This dramatically extends the potential reach of a child's live broadcast beyond the Periscope user base alone, and means that restricting Periscope does not fully address live streaming risks if a child has access to Twitter with live broadcast features enabled.
⚠️ Section 2

Why It Poses Serious Risks for Children

Key risks from live video broadcasting by children, including real-time contact from unknown adults, the permanent nature of broadcast recordings, location exposure, the absence of any content delay or moderation, and the platform's integration with Twitter's broader audience.

🎥
Live Broadcast Exposes Child's Face and Environment in Real Time
A child broadcasting live is visible on camera to an unknown public audience in real time — their appearance, voice, home or school environment, and any identifying details in frame are accessible to anyone watching, including adults with harmful intent
🧑‍💻
Unknown Adults Can Send Messages During Broadcast
The live comment stream allows any viewer — including unknown adults — to send messages directly to a child who is on camera, with no delay and no moderation. Children can be exposed to inappropriate, manipulative or sexually explicit messages while actively broadcasting, with no buffer between sender and recipient
📍
Location Data Can Reveal Where a Child Is
Optional location sharing and environmental details visible in a broadcast can allow viewers to identify where a child is broadcasting from — potentially including their home address or school. Children may not understand the risk of location disclosure during a live stream
💾
Broadcasts Are Permanent and Can Be Saved or Shared
Streams saved as replays create a permanent record of a child's broadcast that can be viewed, saved and distributed by any viewer — long after the child believes the broadcast has ended. Content that a child considered ephemeral in the moment may be preserved and shared without their knowledge or consent
🚫
No Parental Controls or Content Moderation
Neither Periscope nor its Twitter integration offers any parental control features, content filtering for live broadcasts, or pre-broadcast moderation. Parents have no visibility into their child's broadcasting activity and no tools within the platform to restrict or monitor it
👤
No Age Verification for Broadcasters or Viewers
There is no age verification for either broadcasting or viewing on Periscope or via Twitter's live streaming features. Any child can go live in front of a public audience, and any adult can watch and comment on a child's broadcast without any verification process in place
🔴
Periscope Carries a High Risk Rating for Children of All Ages The combination of real-time public broadcasting, live unmoderated comments from unknown adults, location exposure, permanent broadcast recordings and no parental controls makes Periscope and its Twitter-integrated equivalent a platform that poses serious and immediate risks when used by children. Unrestricted access to live broadcasting features should be actively prevented on all devices accessible to children.
🐦
Periscope's Functionality Continues Through Twitter — Restricting Periscope Alone May Not Be Sufficient If a Child Has Twitter Access
Although the standalone Periscope app was officially discontinued in 2021, its live streaming functionality was integrated into Twitter (now X) and continues to be available through that platform. Parents who restrict the Periscope app or website should be aware that a child with access to Twitter on any device may still have access to the same live broadcasting and viewing features that Periscope provided. Comprehensive protection requires addressing both the Periscope brand and Twitter's live streaming features — either by restricting Twitter access as well, or by ensuring that any Twitter account used by the child has appropriate privacy settings applied. iLimits can assist with restricting access to both platforms.
👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
Live Streaming Is Fundamentally Different From Other Social Media — Its Real-Time Nature Creates Risks That Cannot Be Reversed Once a Broadcast Has Started
Unlike posting a photo or video that can be deleted after the fact, a live broadcast cannot be recalled once it is underway. Anything a child shows or says while live on camera is already visible to viewers in real time — and if viewers are recording or screenshotting the stream, its content exists permanently regardless of what the child does after the broadcast ends. This irreversibility makes live streaming a particularly high-stakes activity for children, who may not fully understand that the moment they go live, control over what happens to that content passes largely to their audience. Parents should treat live streaming access as a distinct and serious permission, separate from other social media use.
2
Children May Not Understand the Size or Nature of the Audience Watching a Public Broadcast
Children who use live streaming platforms may conceptualise their audience as similar to their social media followers — people they know, or at least people who are part of their social context. In reality, a public live stream is visible to any member of the platform's user base who discovers it, including adults with no connection to the child. The number of viewers on a live stream can grow rapidly and unpredictably, and the nature of who is watching at any moment is entirely outside the broadcaster's control. Children may also be unaware that their streams are being saved or that viewers can screenshot or record their broadcast without their knowledge.
3
The Periscope Brand Is Discontinued But the Risk It Represents Continues Through Twitter's Live Features
Parents who are aware of Periscope as a platform should know that the standalone app no longer exists as an active service — but the live streaming features it represented are now embedded within Twitter, which remains a major active platform. A child who uses Twitter on any device has access to the same live broadcasting capability that Periscope provided, without needing to download a separate application. Parents managing their child's social media access should consider Twitter's live streaming features alongside the Periscope name when assessing their child's exposure to live broadcasting risks.
4
Restricting Access to Live Streaming Features Is a Meaningful Step Even for Teenagers Who Use Social Media Generally
For families where a teenager has some supervised social media access, live streaming features represent a meaningfully different and higher-risk activity than standard posting and browsing. A teenager who uses Instagram or Twitter for standard social content may not need the same level of unrestricted access to live broadcasting tools that those platforms offer. It is reasonable and appropriate for parents to allow social media access while specifically restricting or limiting live streaming features — and to make that distinction explicit in household agreements about digital use. iLimits provides the tools to apply that distinction at the device and network level.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps to protect children from the specific risks of live video broadcasting — including what conversations to have, what settings to check, and how to address the continuation of Periscope's features within Twitter.

💬
Talk With Your Child Specifically About the Risks of Live Video Broadcasting
Live streaming risks are distinct enough from other online safety topics that they warrant a specific conversation — not just a general reminder about internet safety. Talk with your child about what a public live stream actually means: that anyone can watch, that comments arrive in real time with no filter, that the stream is saved and can be shared, and that anything visible in frame — their face, their room, their school uniform — can be seen by people they do not know. Explain why these features make live broadcasting a different kind of activity from posting a photo, and be clear about your household's rules around live video content. For teenagers, acknowledge that live streaming is a popular activity while being specific about why the risks it carries are serious regardless of age.
🔍
Check Twitter Privacy Settings to Restrict Live Broadcasting If Your Child Uses That Platform
If your child has a Twitter account, review the account's privacy settings to ensure that the account is set to private rather than public, and that live broadcasting features are not enabled or accessible. A private Twitter account limits the audience for any content — including live streams — to approved followers only, which provides a meaningful reduction in risk compared to a public account. Also review which apps have permission to access the Twitter account on your child's device, as third-party applications may have live streaming integrations that extend the same risks through a different interface. Combine in-platform settings with iLimits restrictions for the most complete approach.
🔒
Use iLimits to Block Live Streaming Platforms Across All of Your Child's Devices
Apply iLimits restrictions to block Periscope and related live streaming platforms on every device your child uses — including tablets, secondary phones and shared household devices. Because Periscope's features now exist primarily within Twitter, consider whether Twitter access itself should be restricted or time-limited for younger children. For older teenagers with supervised social media access, a household agreement that specifically excludes live broadcasting — backed by the technical restriction iLimits provides — gives you the most durable and consistent approach. Review restrictions periodically as new live streaming features appear within existing platforms your child uses.
