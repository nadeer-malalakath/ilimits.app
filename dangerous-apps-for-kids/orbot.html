📱 Section 1

What This App Does

A clear and fair picture of Orbot — an open-source app that routes internet traffic through the Tor anonymity network, providing a significantly stronger bypass of network-level monitoring and parental controls than standard VPN tools, and enabling access to .onion sites on the dark web.

🧅
Orbot Routes Traffic Through the Tor Network — Providing Stronger Anonymity Than VPNs and Enabling Dark Web Access on a Child's Device
Orbot is a free, open-source app developed by the Guardian Project that brings the Tor anonymity network to Android devices. Tor routes traffic through a series of volunteer-operated relay nodes around the world, making the traffic exceptionally difficult to trace back to the original device — significantly more so than a standard VPN. On a child's device, Orbot bypasses all network-level parental controls, hides traffic from any monitoring tool, and provides access to .onion sites on the Tor network's hidden services — a part of the internet that is unindexed, unregulated and known to host harmful content alongside its legitimate privacy uses.
🌐
Orbot Is More Than a VPN — It Uses the Tor Network, Which Provides Much Stronger Anonymity and Dark Web Access
Standard VPN tools route traffic through a single external server — the VPN provider can still see the user's traffic, and law enforcement can potentially request that data. Tor routes traffic through multiple relays operated by different parties in different countries, with each relay knowing only the previous and next hop — making the traffic extremely difficult to trace. Additionally, Tor enables access to .onion sites, which are not accessible through standard browsers or VPNs. Both of these features make Orbot a significantly more powerful tool on a child's device than any consumer VPN.

Routes Traffic Through the Tor Network — Multiple Relays in Different Countries
Orbot connects the device to the Tor network, which routes all traffic through a chain of volunteer-operated relay nodes around the world. Unlike a VPN — which sends traffic through a single external server — Tor routes through at least three relays, with each relay knowing only the previous and next hop in the chain. This makes the traffic's origin exceptionally difficult to trace, and it simultaneously bypasses all network-level content filters and parental controls in the same way as a VPN, but with far greater resistance to any monitoring or logging attempt.
🧅
Enables Access to .onion Sites on the Tor Dark Web
Beyond providing anonymous access to the standard internet, Orbot enables access to .onion sites — hidden services on the Tor network that are not accessible through standard browsers, VPNs, or any other tool except a Tor-connected device. The dark web's .onion layer includes legitimate privacy services and activist platforms, but also hosts content that is entirely unavailable on the standard internet precisely because it could not exist there — including illegal marketplaces, extreme content, and harmful material with no equivalent accessibility concern through standard browsers or VPN tools.
🛡️
Developed by the Guardian Project — a Legitimate Human Rights Technology Organisation
Orbot is developed and maintained by the Guardian Project, a non-profit organisation that builds privacy and security tools specifically for human rights activists, journalists and people living under repressive governments. It is associated with the Tor Project, one of the most respected digital rights organisations in the world. Orbot has a genuine and important global purpose — the same technology that makes it a concern on a child's device is what makes it life-saving for journalists in authoritarian contexts. This is the most significant nuance in Orbot's profile and worth reflecting accurately in any conversation with a child about why it is restricted.
📱
Can Route Traffic From All Apps — Not Just a Browser
Orbot can be configured to route traffic from all apps on the device through the Tor network — not just a dedicated browser. This means that messaging apps, social platforms, and other apps a child uses regularly can all have their traffic routed through Tor, bypassing any network-level monitoring that might otherwise track connections from those apps. This all-app routing capability is not available through browser-level VPN tools and significantly extends Orbot's impact on a parent's ability to monitor the device's network activity.
🎓
Technically Advanced — Most Likely to Appear on Devices of Older, Technically Interested Teenagers
Orbot is a more technically complex tool than consumer VPN apps — it is not discovered through casual app store browsing as readily as Turbo VPN or Thunder VPN, and using it effectively requires some understanding of what the Tor network is and how it works. It is most likely to appear on the devices of older teenagers with a specific interest in privacy technology, cybersecurity, or digital rights — a profile similar to F-Droid and ProtonVPN users, but with a more technically demanding tool involved.

What Parents Should Know

1
Orbot Has a Genuine and Important Global Purpose — the Risk on a Child's Device Is About the Technology's Capabilities, Not Its Intentions
The Guardian Project and the Tor Project have built tools that genuinely help journalists, activists and ordinary people living under repressive governments access information and communicate safely. This is a real and important use case, and the technology behind Orbot is exactly as designed. The concern for a child's device is not that Orbot was built for bad reasons — it is that the same capabilities that make it valuable in repressive contexts make it significantly more powerful than any other available tool for bypassing parental controls in a family home, and that those capabilities include access to a part of the internet that has no equivalent concern through any other commonly available app. A conversation with a technically interested child about Orbot should acknowledge this honestly.
2
Orbot Is Significantly More Powerful Than a Consumer VPN — and Finding It on a Device Signals a More Deliberate Choice
A child who has installed Orbot has made a more deliberate and informed choice than one who downloaded Turbo VPN through an ad. Orbot is not a tool you encounter through casual app store browsing — finding it requires knowing what Tor is, why someone would want to use it, and actively seeking it out. A child who has installed Orbot is likely to have a reasonably clear understanding of what it does and why. This does not mean the installation was malicious, but it does mean the conversation about why it is on the device is likely to be more substantive and more informative than a similar conversation about a consumer VPN. Approaching it with genuine curiosity rather than immediate alarm is likely to produce more useful information.
3
The Dark Web Access Concern Is Specific to Orbot — No Other Commonly Available App Provides It
Every other app flagged in this category provides access to the standard internet through an anonymised route — the content available is the same content available through any browser, filtered or otherwise. Orbot is uniquely different in this respect: it enables access to .onion sites on the Tor dark web, which is a distinct layer of the internet accessible only through Tor-connected devices. The dark web includes legitimate privacy services alongside harmful content that is hosted there specifically because it cannot exist on the standard internet. A child using Orbot can reach this layer of the internet with no filtering of any kind applied. This is the specific reason Orbot sits at a higher concern level than consumer VPN tools, regardless of the legitimate purposes the technology also serves.
4
For Technically Interested Teenagers, the Conversation About Tor Should Include Its Global Context — Not Just a Prohibition
A teenager who has found and installed Orbot is likely to know what Tor is and why it was built. A conversation that treats Orbot as simply a bypass tool misses the context that makes the technology significant and that may be part of what drew the child to it. The most durable version of the conversation acknowledges the genuine global importance of Tor and the Guardian Project's work, explains why the same technology creates different concerns in a family home than in a repressive political context, and is direct about the specific additional concern of dark web access that separates Orbot from the consumer VPN tools discussed elsewhere. This is a conversation that treats a technically curious teenager as capable of engaging with nuance — and that is more likely to result in genuine understanding than a prohibition that dismisses the technology's legitimate purpose entirely.

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps for Orbot — including how to approach the conversation with the nuance this specific tool warrants, how to address the dark web concern directly, and how to layer controls effectively.

💬
Acknowledge Tor's Legitimate Purpose — Then Be Direct About the Specific Concern on Their Device
A teenager who has installed Orbot is likely to know what Tor is and why it matters globally. The most effective conversation acknowledges this honestly: Tor is genuinely important for journalists, activists and people in repressive countries — the Guardian Project and the Tor Project do real and valuable work. Then be direct about the two specific concerns on their device. First, it bypasses all household parental controls in the same way as a VPN, but more thoroughly. Second — and this is the concern that makes Orbot categorically different from VPN tools — it enables access to .onion sites on the dark web, which is a part of the internet that includes content with no equivalent accessibility concern through any other available tool. Explaining both concerns clearly, and distinguishing Orbot from consumer VPN tools in the way this distinction deserves, is more likely to produce genuine understanding than either over-reacting or under-reacting to what the technology is.
🧅
Address the Dark Web Access Concern Directly — It Is Specific to Orbot and Not Present With Other Flagged Tools
The dark web access that Orbot enables is different in kind from the standard internet access that VPN tools provide. It is worth being direct with a child about what the dark web actually is — not in sensationalised terms, but accurately: a layer of the internet accessible only through Tor, that includes legitimate privacy services alongside content that cannot exist on the standard internet because it would be removed. A child using Orbot to access .onion sites reaches this layer with no content filtering of any kind applied and no record kept of what was accessed. Most teenagers who have found Orbot through an interest in privacy technology will not be primarily motivated by dark web access, but the conversation about it gives them a clear and specific reason for the restriction that goes beyond the general bypass concern they may already have encountered with VPN tools.
