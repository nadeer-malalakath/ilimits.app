Streamago is a live video streaming application that allows any registered user to broadcast live video of themselves to a public audience. Real-Time Comments and Messages From Viewers During Live Streams
While a child is broadcasting live, any viewer can send real-time comments and messages that appear on screen during the stream. These comments are not pre-moderated and arrive in real time — meaning that inappropriate, sexual, manipulative or distressing messages from adult strangers can appear during a child's live broadcast without any filtering or delay. A child who is live on camera may feel pressure to respond to or engage with comments in real time, creating a dynamic where viewers can direct a child's behaviour during a broadcast in ways that are difficult for the child to recognise as manipulation.

Publicly Discoverable Streams Visible to Any Platform User
Live streams on Streamago are discoverable through the platform's browse interface, which allows any user to find and watch streams by other users. A child who goes live does not need to share a link or invite viewers — their stream will appear in the platform's discovery features and can be found by any user browsing live content. This means that a child who begins broadcasting with the intention of streaming to a small group of friends is in practice broadcasting to the entire platform's user base, with no control over who watches.

Follower Relationships That Enable Ongoing Contact Between Viewers and Broadcasters
Streamago allows viewers to follow broadcasters, creating an ongoing relationship where a follower is notified when the broadcaster goes live again. An adult who watches a child's stream can follow the child, building a pattern of attendance at the child's future broadcasts. This creates a mechanism for ongoing, one-sided attention from unknown adults that the child may experience as flattering engagement from fans, without recognising it as potentially predatory behaviour. The follow relationship also provides a basis for initiating direct contact.
🔓
No Meaningful Age Verification and No Parental Controls
Account creation on Streamago requires only a basic registration with no identity verification or age confirmation beyond a date of birth entry. Children of any age can create accounts, go live and access other users' streams without any effective age check. The platform provides no parental account features, no monitoring tools and no mechanism for a parent to receive notification that their child has begun broadcasting live video to a public audience. A child can go live from their bedroom without a parent being aware that the broadcast is happening.

What Parents Should Know

1
Children May Not Understand That Going Live Means Broadcasting to Complete Strangers
Many children who use live streaming apps do so with the intention of broadcasting to their friends — people they know in real life who follow them on the platform. What they may not fully appreciate is that a live stream on a public platform is visible to the platform's entire user base, not just their followers, and that their followers may include adults they have never met who began following them after discovering their stream through the platform's browse feature. A child who believes they are streaming to a small audience of known friends may in fact be visible to hundreds of adult strangers at any given moment. This misunderstanding of who is watching is one of the most significant risk factors for children on live streaming platforms.
2
Live Broadcasts Can Reveal More Personal Information Than Children Realise
A child broadcasting from their bedroom inadvertently reveals a significant amount of personally identifiable information — the layout of their home, items visible in the background, street or neighbourhood views through windows, school uniforms or sports kit, and the timing of their broadcasts (which can indicate school hours and routines). Adults who watch a child's streams regularly can aggregate this information to build a detailed picture of the child's location, school, home address and daily routine without the child ever having directly shared any of this information. Parents should be aware that a child can expose significant personal safety information through live streaming without having said a single thing that seems like a disclosure.
3
Viewer Flattery and Regular Attendance at a Child's Streams Can Be a Form of Grooming
Children who broadcast regularly on platforms like Streamago often develop a sense of relationship with their regular viewers — people who watch every stream, leave positive comments and make the child feel seen and valued. This dynamic can be genuinely positive among peer-age audiences, but it can also be deliberately cultivated by adults who use regular attendance, flattery and positive reinforcement to build a sense of trust and familiarity with a child before progressing to private contact. A child who has come to think of a regular viewer as a fan or supporter may not recognise an adult's escalating attention as grooming behaviour. Parents should be aware of this pattern and should discuss it with children who use any live streaming platform.
4
Broadcasts Can Be Recorded Without the Child's Knowledge and Shared Elsewhere
Any viewer watching a live stream can use screen recording tools to capture the broadcast without the broadcaster's knowledge or consent. This means that video of a child — including their appearance, voice, home environment and any behaviour during the stream — can be saved and distributed beyond the platform, to other websites or messaging platforms, with no notification to the child or their family. The content of a live stream does not disappear when the broadcast ends — it may persist in recordings made by viewers indefinitely. Parents should ensure that children understand this before they consider using any live streaming platform.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Practical steps to protect your child from the specific risks of live video broadcasting — including conversations about who is really watching, what information a broadcast reveals and why viewer attention is not the same as genuine friendship.

💬
Talk With Your Child About Who Is Really Watching When They Go Live
One of the most important conversations a parent can have with a child who uses or wants to use live streaming platforms is about the reality of who makes up a live audience. Children often imagine streaming to a small group of known followers — but in practice, public live streams are visible to the entire platform, and a child's audience at any moment may include adults they have never met and whose intentions they cannot know. Helping your child understand that going live on a public platform is fundamentally different from video calling friends — and that the audience includes strangers by default, not by exception — is the foundation of safe thinking about live streaming. This conversation should happen before a child ever goes live for the first time, not after.
🌱
Help Your Child Understand What a Broadcast Reveals Without Them Realising
Sit with your child and walk through a hypothetical broadcast from their bedroom or another familiar location, identifying what a stranger watching would be able to learn — visible street signs, recognisable landmarks through a window, school uniform or sports kit, the layout of the home, the time of broadcast. Children are often genuinely surprised by how much information is visible in a background they take for granted. This exercise — done calmly and without framing it as a punishment or accusation — helps children develop a much more concrete sense of what personal safety means in a live video context, and builds habits that will protect them on any platform they use in the future.
🔒
Apply iLimits Restrictions Across Every Device and Keep the Conversation Open
Apply iLimits restrictions for Streamago at the app and browser level across every device your child uses — including shared household tablets, secondary phones and any device they access regularly outside the home. Because live broadcasting can happen very quickly and leave no visible trace on the device, technical controls are particularly important for this category of platform — a brief live session may be over before a parent becomes aware it was happening. Alongside these controls, keep the conversation about live streaming open and non-judgmental, so that your child is more likely to come to you if they receive a message or have an experience during a stream that concerns them.
🔐

Going Live Should Never Mean Going Unprotected

Live video broadcasting puts a child in front of an unknown audience in real time — with no record, no moderation and no way for a parent to know it is happening. The risks are immediate, the exposure is personal and the consequences can be lasting. iLimits gives you the tools to prevent access before a broadcast begins, and the conversations you have with your child about the reality of who watches live streams give them the understanding to protect themselves on every platform they use.
