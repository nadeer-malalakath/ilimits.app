📱 Section 1

What This App Does

A clear picture of ASKfm — one of the original and longest-running anonymous Q&A platforms, which allows anyone to send anonymous questions and messages to a child's named public profile, and which has a longer and more extensively documented history of facilitating serious harm to young users than almost any other platform in this category.

❓
ASKfm Has Been in Operation Since 2010 — and Has the Longest Documented Record of Anonymous Bullying Harm of Any App in This Category
ASKfm is one of the original anonymous Q&A platforms, launched in 2010 and still in operation today. Its core function is allowing anyone to send anonymous questions or messages to a user's public profile. ASKfm has been linked to serious cases of bullying-related harm to young users across multiple countries over more than a decade — incidents that led to widespread media coverage, government investigations and significant regulatory attention in multiple jurisdictions. The platform has introduced moderation measures over the years in response to this pressure, but its fundamental design — anonymous questions to a named user's public profile — has remained unchanged.
📋
ASKfm Has Been Investigated by Governments and Regulators in Multiple Countries — Its History of Harm Is the Longest in the Anonymous Q&A Category
ASKfm attracted sustained regulatory and law enforcement attention from 2012 onwards following high-profile cases in the UK, Ireland, and other countries in which the platform was identified as having been used to harass young users. Parliamentary inquiries, police investigations and significant media coverage followed. The platform was the subject of formal child safety investigations across multiple jurisdictions. This documented regulatory history distinguishes ASKfm from newer entrants to the anonymous messaging category — it is not a platform whose risks are theoretical but one with the most extensively documented record of harm in its category.

Anyone Can Send Anonymous Questions to a Child's Named Public Profile
ASKfm allows any user — logged in or not — to send an anonymous question or message to a child's public profile. The child receives the questions and can respond, with both the anonymous question and their response visible on their public profile to anyone who visits it. This creates a publicly visible record of anonymous messages directed at a specific named child — visible to their peers, classmates, and anyone who searches for their profile — alongside their own responses.
👥
Responses Are Publicly Visible — Creating a Social Pressure to Answer
Unlike some anonymous messaging formats where messages are private to the recipient, ASKfm is designed around a public Q&A feed. When a child answers an anonymous question, the exchange is posted to their public profile. This creates a social dynamic where a child may feel compelled to respond to questions — including inappropriate, hostile or distressing ones — because not responding is also visible. The public nature of the format amplifies the impact of hostile questions by making them visible to the child's entire social audience.
📋
Extensively Documented Record of Facilitating Harm to Young Users
ASKfm's record of harm is more extensively documented than any other app in the anonymous social category. High-profile cases in the UK, Ireland and elsewhere linked ASKfm to serious harm suffered by young users who had been subjected to sustained anonymous bullying through the platform. The cases attracted parliamentary-level attention, formal regulatory investigations, and sustained media scrutiny over several years beginning in 2012. The platform has updated its moderation practices and safety features in response to this pressure, but the fundamental anonymous-questions-to-public-profile design that created the conditions for harm has remained its core feature.
🌍
Global Platform — Anonymous Questions Can Come From Anywhere
Unlike location-based anonymous platforms, ASKfm operates globally — anonymous questions can be sent from anyone, anywhere in the world, who can find a child's profile. A child with a discoverable public profile on ASKfm can receive anonymous questions from completely unknown individuals with no geographic or social connection to them, in addition to questions from peers who know them. This extends the potential sender pool beyond a child's local social circle to an entirely unlimited global audience.
🔗
Profiles Are Shareable and Linkable — Amplifying Exposure
ASKfm profiles and individual question-answer exchanges can be linked and shared through other platforms. A hostile or embarrassing exchange on a child's ASKfm profile can be screenshotted and shared through social media, messaging apps or other channels — amplifying the content beyond ASKfm itself and creating a persistent public record of the anonymous harassment. The shareable nature of public Q&A profiles means that harmful content posted to ASKfm can spread to communities outside the platform.

👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
ASKfm's History Sets It Apart From Other Anonymous Q&A Apps — It Is Not a Newly Emerged Risk
Many parents who encounter ASKfm on a child's device may be more familiar with the platform's reputation from a decade ago than from recent experience. It is worth knowing that ASKfm is still active, still used by teenagers, and that its fundamental design has not meaningfully changed despite years of safety-related pressure. The platform introduced moderation tools and reporting mechanisms following the regulatory attention it received, but these do not address the core mechanism — anonymous targeted messages to a named public profile — that created the conditions for harm in the first place. ASKfm's history is not a reason to treat it as a historical problem that has been resolved; it is the context that makes its continued use by children a well-evidenced rather than theoretical concern.
2
The Public Profile Format Adds a Dimension of Harm Not Present in Private Anonymous Messaging Apps
Unlike NGL, where anonymous messages are received privately, ASKfm's anonymous questions are received and responded to publicly. This means a child's entire social audience — and anyone who finds the profile through a search — can see both the anonymous questions directed at them and their responses. A hostile or mocking question that a child responds to becomes part of a publicly indexed record. The child may feel pressure to respond normally to hostile questions to avoid drawing attention to the fact they received them. The public nature of the format makes the harm more visible to peers and more difficult to contain than private anonymous messaging.
3
If Your Child Has an ASKfm Profile, Check Whether It Is Public and What It Contains
If ASKfm is found on a child's device, check whether they have a profile and, if so, whether it is set to public or private. A public ASKfm profile is searchable and viewable by anyone, and may contain a record of anonymous questions the child has received alongside their responses — including potentially harmful content the child did not disclose. Reviewing the profile can give useful information about what kinds of questions were received, how long the profile has been active, and whether any of the content requires follow-up conversation. The profile should be reviewed sensitively, as its contents may include material the child found distressing that they did not feel able to bring to a parent.
4
The Same Category of Appeal Applies Here as With NGL — Start With Understanding Why the Child Was Using It
Like NGL and other anonymous Q&A formats, ASKfm is used by teenagers who want to know what their peers think of them — a normal adolescent desire for social feedback and validation. The conversation about ASKfm is most productive when it begins by acknowledging this motivation honestly, then explains why the specific format creates conditions where anonymous feedback is more likely to be harmful than helpful. A child who used ASKfm was responding to a genuine social desire — not making a reckless choice — and the restriction is easier to accept when it is explained in terms of the format's specific problems rather than as a general prohibition on social apps.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps for ASKfm — including how to review an existing public profile, how to approach the conversation with context from the platform's history, and how category-level blocking covers ASKfm alongside its successors.

🔍
Check Whether a Public ASKfm Profile Exists and What It Contains
If ASKfm is found on a child's device, the first practical step is reviewing whether they have a profile and what it contains. ASKfm profiles are public by default and searchable online — you can view the profile's public content without the child's device. Check whether the profile is set to public or private, how long it has been active, and what kinds of anonymous questions it has received alongside the child's responses. Review the content sensitively: the profile may contain material the child found distressing that they have not disclosed, and approaching the review as information-gathering rather than surveillance is more likely to support a productive follow-up conversation. If the profile contains content that constitutes harassment or threatening material, it can be reported through the platform and, where appropriate, to school administration or law enforcement.
💬
Have the Same Conversation as for NGL — With Context From ASKfm's Documented History
The conversation about ASKfm follows the same framework as the NGL conversation: acknowledge the legitimate desire for peer feedback and validation that anonymous Q&A platforms exploit, explain why the anonymous format specifically produces conditions more likely to generate harmful messages than helpful ones, and address directly the specific harm that a public profile adds compared to private anonymous messaging. For ASKfm specifically, there is one additional element worth including: the platform's documented history. ASKfm is not a new app whose risks are theoretical — it is an app that attracted formal government and law enforcement attention over years of documented harm to young users. This context gives the restriction a basis in public record rather than parental concern alone, which may make it easier for a teenager to accept as a considered rather than arbitrary decision.
🔒
Block ASKfm at the Category Level and Deactivate Any Existing Profile Before Restriction Takes Effect
Apply iLimits category-level restrictions to block ASKfm alongside NGL and equivalent anonymous Q&A apps in a single action. Enable Family Link's app installation approval to generate alerts for future installation attempts. Before or alongside the restriction, consider deactivating any existing ASKfm profile — an active public profile continues to be accessible and sendable by others even if the child no longer has the app installed, because ASKfm is also accessible through a web browser. Deactivating or deleting the profile removes the anonymous inbound message channel entirely, not just the app. To deactivate: log into the profile and use the account settings to deactivate or delete the account. If the child's profile is public, also consider whether a Google search of their username shows the profile in results — deactivating the account does not immediately remove search engine indexing of previously public content, and this may take additional time.
