A child's complete browsing activity passes through these servers, and there is no way to verify whether those servers are logging the traffic, injecting advertisements, modifying content, or engaging in other practices that may affect both the child's privacy and the safety of the content they receive.

App-level proxy tools are more effective at removing browsing history entirely than web-based proxy sites.
👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
Proxy Apps and VPN Apps Are the Same Category of Risk — Both Bypass Network-Level Controls
The technical difference between a proxy app and a VPN app — the level of encryption applied to the traffic and the routing method used — is not relevant to the child safety concern. Both bypass network-level content filters, both hide browsing destinations from monitoring tools, and both allow access to content that household controls are designed to restrict. A parent who has blocked VPN tools but not proxy tools has a gap in their coverage. iLimits addresses both categories in a single category-level restriction, which is the most practical approach given the number of individual apps in each.
2
Free Proxy Apps Route a Child's Traffic Through Servers That Cannot Be Verified
One of the specific concerns with free proxy apps that distinguishes them from named commercial VPN services is the complete absence of information about the servers handling the traffic. A child whose browsing is routed through a free proxy app's public server pool is sending their web requests — including any typed information in forms, login details on sites, and browsing history — through a server operated by a party whose identity, location, and data practices are entirely unknown. This is not a hypothetical risk: public proxy servers have been used to inject advertising, harvest credentials, and modify web content. Parents should understand that "free and easy to use" in the proxy context often means "operated by someone with an undisclosed motivation for providing free internet routing."
3
A Child Using a Proxy App Was Trying to Bypass Something Specific
Like VPN tools, proxy apps are specifically sought by people who know they want to bypass a network restriction. A child who has downloaded and activated a proxy app was motivated by something they believed was blocked — a platform, a category of content, or a specific website. Understanding what that was gives more useful information than removing the app alone. The conversation that begins with genuine curiosity about what they were trying to access is more likely to address the actual situation than a conversation focused purely on the tool.
4
Browser History May Still Contain Partial Information — Proxy Apps Are Not as Thorough as VPNs at Removing All Traces
Unlike VPN apps, which encrypt all traffic and leave network monitoring tools entirely blind, basic proxy apps may not encrypt the traffic they route and may not prevent the browser from recording the visited URLs in its history. A parent who suspects a proxy app is being used should check the device's browser history — the proxy server's own address will appear, and depending on the proxy's implementation, the sites visited through it may also appear. Network monitoring tools at the router level may also show connection destinations that a basic proxy does not fully conceal. This partial visibility is a useful compensating control that does not apply to VPN tools.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps for Proxy Server — addressing the bypass concern, the unknown server quality, and the motivation behind its use, alongside the most effective controls to put in place.

💬
Address Both the Bypass and the Unknown Server Risk in Conversation
A conversation about proxy apps has two useful angles. The first is the same as for VPN tools: explain what a proxy does to household network controls and why that matters. The second is specific to free proxy apps: explain that unlike a named VPN service — where at least some public information about the provider exists — a free proxy app routes your child's browsing through servers run by parties whose identity is completely unknown, and whose motivation for providing free internet routing is undisclosed. Help your child understand that the question "whose server is handling my browsing?" is not paranoid — it is the right question to ask about any service that offers to route your internet traffic for free. For a child who has found the proxy app through a simple search for "how to unblock [something]", this is likely to be a more informative conversation than the technical explanation of how proxy routing works.
🔍
Check the Browser History and Router Logs for Context on What Was Being Accessed
Unlike VPN tools, basic proxy apps may leave partial traces. Check the device's browser history for proxy server addresses and for any sites that may have appeared directly despite the proxy use. If your household router has activity logging enabled, check the connection logs for the period when the proxy was active — basic proxy apps may not fully conceal destination addresses at the network level in the same way a fully encrypted VPN does. This gives useful context for understanding what was being accessed and whether the proxy was used occasionally for a specific purpose or regularly as a persistent bypass. Understanding the pattern of use helps calibrate the response and the conversation.
🔒
Block the Proxy and VPN Category Together, Enable Family Link Approval, and Layer Device With Network Controls
Apply iLimits restrictions at the proxy and VPN category level — this covers Proxy Server alongside VPN tools and similar bypass apps in a single action. Enable Google Family Link's app installation approval requirement to generate an alert before any new proxy app is installed. Combine device-level controls with network-level DNS filtering: device-level controls block proxy apps from running, while DNS-based content filters address content access from devices and browsing contexts not under direct device management. Also consider enabling DNS-based blocking of known web proxy sites at the router level as a complementary layer — while no DNS block list is comprehensive, blocking the most commonly used web proxy sites adds friction that may be sufficient for casual bypass attempts.
