What This Platform Does

A clear picture of GroupMe — a group messaging service owned by Microsoft that allows users to create and join group chats of any size, send direct messages, and share media, with no minimum age requirement and no parental controls of any kind.

💬
GroupMe Is a Group Messaging App With No Parental Controls, No Content Moderation and No Meaningful Age Restriction
GroupMe is a group messaging application owned by Microsoft, widely used in schools, universities and social groups to organise communication among large numbers of people. While it is most commonly associated with college-age users, there is no minimum age requirement enforced on the platform and no parental control features of any kind. Children can join groups created by anyone, receive direct messages from unknown users, and access or share any content sent within a group chat — all without any moderation, filtering or parental visibility. The absence of any child safety features makes GroupMe a platform that requires deliberate parental attention when used by younger children and teenagers.

GroupMe is a group and direct messaging platform that allows users to create group chats of up to 5,000 members, send direct messages to individual contacts, share photos, videos, documents and links, and receive messages via SMS as well as through the app. The platform requires only an email address or phone number to register, with no age verification of any kind.
👥
Large Group Chats With No Membership Restrictions
GroupMe's core feature is the ability to create and join group chats of up to 5,000 members. Groups can be created by anyone and joined via a shared link, meaning a child can be added to or join a group whose other members are entirely unknown to them or their parents. The size and open nature of groups means that the content and participants in any given chat can be entirely outside a parent's awareness — a child may be participating in large group conversations with adults, older teenagers or people with no connection to their school or social circle.
📩
Direct Messaging Between Any Two Users
In addition to group chats, GroupMe supports one-to-one direct messaging between any two users on the platform. A child who has their phone number or username can be contacted directly by any other user — including adults with no legitimate connection to the child. Unlike some platforms, GroupMe does not restrict who can send a direct message based on any prior connection, and there is no option for a parent to limit or review direct message contacts.
🖼️
Unrestricted Media Sharing Including Images and Video
GroupMe allows users to share photos, videos, documents and links within both group chats and direct messages. There is no content moderation applied to media shared within chats — no scanning for explicit content, no filtering of inappropriate images or video, and no restriction on the type of material that can be sent. A child participating in a group chat has no protection against being sent explicit, disturbing or age-inappropriate media by other group members.
📱
SMS Integration Means Messages Arrive Even Without the App Open
GroupMe integrates with SMS, meaning that messages sent in a group or direct conversation can be delivered as standard text messages to a child's phone — even if the GroupMe app is not installed or is restricted. This SMS delivery feature bypasses app-level controls, meaning that restricting or removing the GroupMe app from a device does not necessarily prevent a child from receiving messages sent through the platform if their phone number is associated with a GroupMe account.
🔗
Group Joining via Shareable Links With No Approval Required
GroupMe groups can be shared via a link that anyone with the link can use to join the group immediately, without requiring approval from the group creator or any existing members. This means a child can join a group of entirely unknown adults simply by tapping a link shared in another platform or message. Parents may have no awareness that their child has joined a new group, who the other members are, or what content is being shared within it.

👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
GroupMe Is Most Commonly Used by Teenagers and Young Adults — But Has No Features Designed to Protect Younger Users
GroupMe is particularly common among secondary school and university students as a tool for organising class groups, sports teams, clubs and social gatherings. This means that many children encounter the platform for the first time through a legitimate school or extracurricular context. However, the platform's complete absence of child safety features means that even a legitimately created school group sits alongside direct messaging and group joining features that carry real risks. Parents should distinguish between the specific group their child was invited to join and the broader platform capabilities that child now has access to as a registered user.
2
A Child May Have a GroupMe Account Without a Parent Being Aware of It
Because GroupMe requires only a phone number or email address to register — with no age verification and no parental notification — children can create accounts independently without a parent's knowledge. The app may not be immediately visible if a child has placed it in a folder or if the parent does not regularly review their child's installed applications. Parents who want to know whether their child is using GroupMe should check both the installed apps on their child's device and whether their child is receiving GroupMe-originated SMS messages, which will often include GroupMe branding or be from an unfamiliar number.
3
The Groups a Child Belongs to Matter As Much as Whether They Use the Platform
GroupMe's risk level varies significantly depending on which groups a child is part of. A closed, school-administered group for a specific class is a very different environment from a large open group joined via a link shared on another platform. Parents whose children use GroupMe for legitimate purposes — such as a school sports team chat — should be aware of which groups their child belongs to and who the other members of those groups are. Periodic conversations about group membership, and an agreement that the child will not join new groups without a parent's knowledge, are among the most practical steps available in the absence of any platform-level controls.
4
Blocking the App Alone May Not Be Sufficient — GroupMe's SMS Integration Requires Additional Consideration
GroupMe's integration with SMS means that messages sent through the platform can arrive as standard text messages on a child's phone even if the GroupMe app is restricted or removed. Parents who block app access via iLimits should also be aware of this delivery mechanism. If a child's phone number is registered with a GroupMe account, messages from any group they belong to may continue to arrive as texts. Deactivating the GroupMe account associated with the child's phone number — rather than simply restricting app access — may be necessary for complete prevention of message delivery where that is the parent's intention.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps to help you manage your child's use of GroupMe safely — including what to discuss, what to check, and how to apply the right technical controls given the platform's unique SMS delivery behaviour.

💬
Talk With Your Child About Which Groups They Belong to and Who the Other Members Are
Because GroupMe has no parental visibility features, conversation with your child is one of the most important tools available. Ask your child to show you which GroupMe groups they are part of, who created each group and who the other members are. Agree on a household rule that they will not join any new group without telling you first — and explain the reason for that rule in practical terms: that groups can contain people they do not know, and that content shared in large groups is outside anyone's control. For younger children, consider asking that GroupMe use be confined to specific, parent-approved groups rather than open access to all platform features.
🔍
Check Whether Your Child Has a GroupMe Account and Review Their Group Memberships
If you are not certain whether your child has a GroupMe account, check for the app on their device and look for GroupMe-branded SMS messages in their text message inbox — the platform often delivers messages as texts from unfamiliar numbers with GroupMe branding. If your child does have an account, ask them to log in while you are with them so you can review their group memberships and direct message contacts together. This is not a punitive exercise — frame it as a regular check-in about their digital communication in the same way you might ask about their social life more broadly.
🔒
Use iLimits to Restrict App Access, and Consider Account Deactivation for Full Message Prevention
Apply iLimits restrictions to block the GroupMe app and web interface on your child's devices. If your intention is to prevent all GroupMe messaging — rather than to manage access by time or context — also consider deactivating the GroupMe account associated with your child's phone number, since the platform's SMS integration means that messages can continue to arrive as standard texts even when the app is blocked. Account deactivation can be done from within the GroupMe app settings or by contacting GroupMe support, and is the most complete way to prevent message delivery where full restriction is the goal.
