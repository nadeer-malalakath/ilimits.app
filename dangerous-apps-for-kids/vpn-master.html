Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps for addressing VPN Master — including why category-level blocking matters, how to explain what a VPN actually does to a child, and how to layer device and network controls for the most complete protection.

💬
Explain That VPNs Shift Who Sees the Traffic — They Don't Make It Invisible
The most useful conversation about VPN tools focuses on what they actually do with a child's internet traffic rather than just setting a rule about them. Help your child understand the practical reality: when they connect to VPN Master, their traffic stops being visible to the household network and becomes visible instead to VPN Master's servers — servers operated by a company they know very little about. For a free service that has attracted scrutiny from privacy researchers, this is not a neutral trade. "Private" in the marketing sense means private from the household network; it does not mean private from the VPN provider. A child who understands this distinction — that VPN use relocates visibility rather than removing it — is better equipped to think critically about the privacy claims of VPN products they encounter, and more likely to come to a parent with questions rather than simply using whatever tool their peers recommend.
🗂️
Block the VPN Category — Not Just VPN Master
VPN Master is one app in a category that includes dozens of functionally equivalent alternatives, all available on Google Play, all free to use, and all carrying the same core risk of bypassing network-level parental controls. Blocking VPN Master individually without addressing the category is an incomplete response — a child who finds VPN Master blocked can switch to Turbo VPN, Hotspot Shield, Thunder VPN or any of a number of alternatives within a minute. Apply iLimits restrictions at the category level to block the consumer VPN and proxy tool category as a whole. This provides significantly more durable and complete coverage than addressing individual apps, and it remains effective when new VPN apps are released or recommended to your child.
🔒
Combine Device-Level Controls With Network-Level Filtering for Layered Protection
No single layer of parental control is complete on its own, and VPN tools are the most direct illustration of why. Network-level filters are effective for the vast majority of content risk scenarios, but any VPN app makes them ineffective while it is running. Device-level controls — such as those iLimits provides — operate independently of network routing and are not bypassed by VPN traffic. The most resilient setup combines both layers: iLimits at the device level to prevent VPN tools from running, and network-level controls as a secondary layer covering devices and traffic not under direct device-level management. Review your current configuration with this layering in mind, and ensure Family Link is set to require approval for all Play Store installations so that any attempt to install a VPN app triggers a notification before the installation succeeds.
