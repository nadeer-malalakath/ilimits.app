What This Platform Does

A clear picture of Life360 — a family location-sharing app that continuously tracks the real-time GPS location of every family member who has the app installed, along with driving behaviour, place history and other personal data, much of which is shared with third parties.

📍
Life360 Is a Continuous Location Tracking Service — Its Concerns Are About Privacy and Data Practices, Not Third-Party Contact
Life360 is different from every other platform in this series. It does not expose children to strangers, adult content or unmoderated communities. Its purpose is family location sharing — parents installing it on a child's phone to track their whereabouts in real time. iLimits flags Life360 not because it connects children with dangers from outside the family, but because of documented concerns about the platform's commercial data practices, the privacy implications of continuous precise location tracking, and the well-documented impact that covert or non-consensual surveillance can have on the parent-child relationship, particularly for teenagers. These are concerns worth understanding before deciding whether and how to use the app.

Life360 is a continuous family location tracking application that monitors the real-time GPS position of every enrolled family member, logs location history, detects driving behaviour and sends alerts when family members arrive at or leave designated places. The app is widely marketed as a family safety tool — but its data practices, its commercial model and its psychological impact on children and teenagers have been the subject of significant concern from privacy researchers, child development experts and investigative journalists.
📡
Continuous Real-Time GPS Location Tracking of All Family Members
Life360 continuously tracks the precise GPS location of every family member with the app installed, updating their position on a shared family map in real time. This means that a parent with Life360 installed on a child's phone can see exactly where the child is at any moment of any day — their current street, their speed of travel and how long they have been at any given location. The app runs in the background continuously, including at school, at friends' homes, during extracurricular activities and in any other setting, without requiring the child to take any action to share their location.
🗓️
Detailed Location History Logs Retained Over Time
Life360 does not only show where a family member is now — it retains a detailed log of everywhere they have been, accessible to other family members through the app. This history includes specific addresses, timestamps and duration of stay at every location the child has visited. Location history logs create a detailed record of a child's social life, routine, relationships and movements that goes far beyond what most parents would have access to through normal family communication, and that the child may not know is being retained and reviewable.
🚗
Driving Behaviour Monitoring Including Speed and Phone Use Detection
Life360's premium tiers include monitoring of driving behaviour — detecting speed, hard braking, rapid acceleration and phone use while driving. While this feature is presented as a road safety tool, it also means that the app collects and retains detailed behavioural data about a child's driving patterns over time, which is stored on Life360's servers and subject to the platform's data sharing practices alongside location data.
🏷️
Place Alerts That Notify Parents of Arrivals and Departures
Parents can designate specific locations — home, school, a friend's house — and receive automatic notifications when a family member arrives at or departs from these places. While this feature has legitimate safety applications, it also means that a child's movements trigger automatic notifications to a parent without the child taking any action or having any awareness of when those notifications are sent. A child using Life360 may not know at any given moment whether their current location has triggered a parental alert.
💼
Commercial Data Sharing Practices That Extend Beyond the Family Circle
Life360 has been the subject of significant investigative journalism and regulatory attention regarding its practice of selling precise location data collected from its users — including data collected from children — to third-party data brokers. Investigations have found that this data, while technically anonymised, can often be de-anonymised using other available information. The commercial value of Life360's service is partly derived from the location data it collects, meaning that families using the app are not simply using a safety tool — they are contributing to a commercial data collection operation whose downstream uses they cannot control or audit.

👨‍👩‍👧 Section 3

What Parents Should Know

1
Life360 Has Sold Precise Location Data Collected From Its Users — Including Children — to Data Brokers
In 2021, investigative reporting established that Life360 was selling the precise location data of its users to a range of commercial data brokers — companies that aggregate and resell location data to advertisers, insurers, hedge funds and other commercial buyers. This data included location records collected from children's devices. Life360 subsequently announced changes to some of its data-sharing practices, but parents should be aware that a platform marketed as a family safety tool has a commercial history of monetising the location data it collects from families, including their children. Reviewing Life360's current privacy policy and data-sharing disclosures before installation is strongly recommended.
2
Continuous Tracking Can Damage Teenage Wellbeing and the Parent-Child Relationship
A growing body of research from child development specialists and adolescent psychologists has examined the impact of parental location tracking on teenagers. The findings are nuanced but consistent on several points: teenagers who are tracked continuously and without genuine consent report higher levels of anxiety and lower levels of trust in their parents; teenagers who discover covert tracking are significantly more likely to find technological workarounds and significantly less likely to communicate openly about their whereabouts and activities; and the development of healthy independence in adolescence — which requires the experience of managing one's own movements and decisions — can be impeded by continuous real-time surveillance. These are not arguments against all forms of parental oversight, but they are important context for understanding what continuous tracking does and does not achieve.
3
Transparent Use With a Child's Knowledge and Agreement Produces Better Outcomes Than Covert Installation
Studies of family tracking apps consistently find that the same tool produces meaningfully different outcomes depending on how it is introduced and used. When Life360 is installed transparently — with the child's knowledge of what it does, an honest conversation about why the parent wants to use it, and agreed boundaries around when and how location data will be checked — it is associated with better safety outcomes and less damage to the parent-child relationship than when it is installed covertly. If you decide to use Life360, involving your child in that decision, explaining the reasons honestly and agreeing clear rules about how the data will be used is not just advisable — it is likely to make the app more effective as a safety tool.
4
Other Approaches to Family Safety May Achieve Similar Goals With Fewer Privacy Trade-Offs
For families whose primary interest is knowing that a child has arrived safely at a destination or being reachable in an emergency, there are approaches that achieve similar safety outcomes without the continuous data collection and commercial data-sharing practices of Life360. Agreed check-in messages, native device location-sharing features offered by Apple and Google that do not involve a third-party data collector, and open conversations about expected whereabouts are all approaches that parents may wish to consider alongside or instead of a commercial tracking service. iLimits does not prescribe the right approach for any individual family — but it does encourage parents to make that decision with full awareness of the trade-offs involved.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Practical steps for families considering Life360 — covering how to approach the conversation with your child, what to review before installation and how to use location tools in a way that supports rather than undermines your family's safety and trust.

💬
Have the Conversation Before You Install — Not After
If you are considering installing Life360 on your child's device, the single most important step you can take is to have an honest conversation with your child before you do so — not after, and not in a way that frames the app as something being done to them without their input. Explain what the app does, why you think it would be useful, what you intend to use the data for and what you will not use it for. Invite your child's response. You do not need your child's permission to install a monitoring app — but involving them in the decision, and giving them a genuine voice in how it is used, will significantly improve both the safety outcomes and the effect on your relationship. A child who agrees to transparent tracking is in a much stronger position than one who discovers covert tracking and concludes they cannot trust their parent.
🔍
Review Life360's Current Privacy Policy and Data-Sharing Practices Before Installation
Before installing Life360, read the platform's current privacy policy and data-sharing disclosures carefully. Life360 has updated its practices following investigative reporting and regulatory pressure, but the specifics of what data is collected, how long it is retained and what third parties it may be shared with remain important information for parents to understand before enrolling their family on the platform. Pay particular attention to data relating to children, opt-out provisions and what happens to collected data if you cancel your subscription. Making an informed decision about whether the platform's current data practices are acceptable to your family is a basic step that many parents skip because the app is marketed as a safety tool rather than a data collection service — but it remains both categories.
🌱
Consider Whether Agreed Check-Ins or Native Device Tools Meet Your Safety Needs Without the Trade-Offs
For many families, the safety goal that Life360 is intended to serve — knowing that a child has arrived somewhere safely and being able to reach them in an emergency — can be met through less data-intensive approaches. Apple's Find My and Google's native location-sharing features offer real-time location sharing without a commercial third party collecting and potentially monetising that data. Agreed check-in messages — a simple text when a child arrives at a destination — build communication habits that remain useful long after a child no longer lives at home. If your primary goal is emergency safety rather than continuous oversight, these alternatives are worth considering alongside Life360 before deciding which approach best fits your family's values and your child's stage of development.
🔐

Informed Decisions About Tracking Are as Important as the Tracking Itself

Keeping children safe requires good information — about the platforms they use, and about the tools parents use to monitor them. Life360 can be a genuine safety asset for some families, used transparently and with full awareness of its data practices. For others, the privacy concerns and the wellbeing research may point toward different approaches. iLimits gives you the information to make that decision clearly, and the tools to implement whatever approach your family decides is right.
