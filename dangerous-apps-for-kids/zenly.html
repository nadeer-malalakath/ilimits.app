Zenly Was a Real-Time Location Sharing App Designed Around Continuous, Always-On GPS Tracking Between Friends — With No Parental Controls and Significant Social Pressure to Stay Visible
Zenly was a location sharing application owned by Snap Inc. that allowed users to share their precise GPS location with friends on a continuous, real-time basis — not just for a specific journey or event, but as a persistent, always-on feed of where each person was at any moment. The app was popular among teenagers and included features that revealed not just location but battery percentage, speed of travel, and how recently the location had been updated. Zenly was officially shut down by Snap in February 2023, but the category of app it represented — always-on social location sharing — continues through successor apps and features built into other platforms. Parents whose children were Zenly users, or who are now using similar location-sharing apps, should understand what this category of application does and why it requires careful consideration.

🗺️
Continuous Real-Time GPS Location Visible to All Friends
Zenly's core feature was a shared map showing the precise GPS location of every friend in a user's network, updated continuously and viewable at any time. Unlike location sharing features in other apps that require an active decision to share for a specific trip or period, Zenly's sharing was always-on by default — a child's location was visible to everyone in their friend list at every moment of the day and night, without the child needing to take any active step to share it. This persistent, passive visibility is the defining feature that makes always-on location sharing apps a distinct category of risk.
🔋
Passive Data Including Battery Level, Speed and Last-Seen Time
In addition to GPS position, Zenly surfaced each user's battery percentage, their speed of movement, and precisely how long ago their location was last updated. This combination of data allowed anyone in a user's friend network to infer detailed information about their daily patterns — whether they were at home, travelling, awake or asleep, and whether their phone was running low on charge. For a child, this level of passive data visibility to a network of contacts represents a significant privacy exposure that goes well beyond simple location sharing.
👥
Social Layer Built Around Location Visibility and Presence
Zenly was designed as a social application, not merely a utility — the shared map was a social space where users could interact with each other's locations, leave comments tied to places, and build a shared picture of their social world through the lens of where everyone was at any given moment. This social dimension created pressure dynamics: a child who turned off location sharing or appeared to be hiding their location could face social questions or accusations from friends, making the decision to stop sharing feel socially costly in ways that abstract privacy concerns rarely do for teenagers.
🔒
No Parental Visibility, Controls or Account Linking Features
Zenly provided no parental control features of any kind — no family account linking, no ability for a parent to see their child's friend list on the platform, no activity reporting and no option to review or approve the contacts who could see a child's location. A parent whose child used Zenly had no visibility into who was tracking their child's movements in real time, and no tools within the platform to manage or restrict that access. External controls and direct conversation with the child were the only available approach.
🔄
Discontinued in 2023 — But Successor Apps Fill the Same Category
Snap shut down Zenly in February 2023, but the always-on social location sharing category the app represented has not disappeared. Several successor applications emerged in its wake — including Snapchat's own location sharing features, Life360, Beacon, and various other apps — that offer substantially the same functionality. Parents whose children used Zenly may find that they have migrated to one of these alternatives, often without the transition being discussed or the new app being subject to the same level of parental awareness as the original.

Why It Requires Parental Awareness

Key risks associated with always-on social location sharing apps, including physical safety implications of continuous location visibility, social pressure dynamics that make opting out difficult, the absence of parental controls, and the migration of Zenly users to successor platforms.

📍
Continuous Location Visible to an Unvetted Friend Network
A child's precise GPS location, updated in real time throughout the day and night, was visible to everyone in their Zenly friend list — which may have included people the parent had never met, older contacts from online communities, or individuals the child had lost touch with but never removed from the app
🏠
Home Address and Daily Routine Exposed by Movement Patterns
A persistent location feed effectively reveals a child's home address, school, regular routes and daily patterns to everyone who can see their location — information that could be misused by any contact whose intentions toward the child are not entirely benign, including older contacts or adults who entered the friend network through indirect social connections
😰
Social Pressure to Remain Visible at All Times
The social design of apps like Zenly creates significant peer pressure to keep location sharing active — turning it off, or appearing to hide one's location, can trigger social friction or accusations among teenage peer groups, making privacy protection feel socially costly in ways that are difficult for younger users to navigate independently
🕵️
Enables Monitoring and Control by Peers or Older Contacts
Always-on location sharing can be exploited by controlling peers, ex-partners or older contacts to monitor a child's movements and whereabouts without explicit consent at the time of each check-in — a dynamic that has been associated with controlling relationship behaviours among teenagers
🔄
Migration to Successor Apps May Go Unnoticed
Children who used Zenly often migrated to successor applications when it shut down, sometimes without discussing the transition with parents. A parent who is aware of Zenly as a risk may be unaware that their child has moved to a functionally identical alternative and is sharing their location continuously through a different app name
🚫
No Parental Controls in the App or Its Successors
Zenly had no parental control features, and most of its successor applications in the social location sharing category similarly provide no tools for parents to monitor, restrict or review their child's use — meaning that external device-level controls are the primary available safeguard

What Parents Should Know

1
Always-On Location Sharing Is Categorically Different From Sharing Location for a Specific Trip or Event
Many parents are comfortable with the idea of knowing where their child is — and many families use location sharing for exactly this purpose, as a safety tool that works in both directions. Always-on social location sharing apps like Zenly are a different thing: the sharing is not between parent and child for safety, but between a child and their entire peer network for social purposes, continuously and passively, without any active decision being made each time. The distinction matters because it changes who has access to the location data, how that data can be used, and what social dynamics it creates. Parents who are comfortable with family location sharing should still give careful consideration to peer-facing always-on location sharing, which serves a different purpose and carries different risks.
2
The Friend Networks on These Apps Are Often Less Well Known to Parents Than Those on Other Social Platforms
Location sharing apps tend to have friend networks that reflect a teenager's social world in full — including contacts from school year groups, sports teams, online communities and other social contexts that a parent may have limited visibility into. Unlike a parent who reviews their child's Instagram followers, a parent may have no awareness of who is in their child's Zenly or successor app contact list, and therefore no basis for assessing whether any of those contacts having access to the child's real-time location is appropriate. Asking to see the contact list on any location sharing app a child uses is a reasonable and important step for parents.
3
Teenagers May Not Perceive the Privacy Risk Because the Sharing Feels Reciprocal and Social
One of the features that made Zenly and similar apps appealing to teenagers is that the location sharing is mutual — everyone can see everyone else, which makes it feel like a shared, social activity rather than surveillance. This reciprocity makes it harder for teenagers to perceive the privacy risk, because the app is framed around connection and presence rather than tracking. Parents should acknowledge this when talking to teenagers about the app — the issue is not that sharing location with close friends is inherently wrong, but that the friend network is broader than close friends, that the data is more detailed than a simple location pin, and that the always-on nature removes the active consent that characterises safer forms of location sharing.
4
Location Sharing Features Are Embedded in Many Other Apps — The Risk Does Not Require a Dedicated Location App
Snapchat's Snap Map feature, Instagram's location sharing, Find My Friends on iOS, and various other platforms all include location sharing features that carry some of the same considerations as dedicated location apps. A parent who blocks dedicated location sharing apps should also be aware that a child may have access to equivalent features within social platforms they already use. Reviewing the location permissions and location features within every social app on a child's device — not just dedicated location apps — is the most complete approach to managing this risk category.

💡 Section 6

Guidance for Parents

Three practical steps for managing your child's use of location sharing apps — including how to have the conversation, what to check on their device, and how to think about the broader category of location-sharing features across all the platforms they use.

💬
Talk With Your Child About Who Can See Their Location and Why That Matters
The most effective way to approach location sharing apps with teenagers is to make the conversation about the specific details rather than the general principle. Ask your child to show you their contact list on any location sharing app they use and talk through who each person is — not as an interrogation, but as a genuine conversation about whether every person on that list having access to their real-time location at any moment makes sense. Acknowledge that sharing location with close friends can feel like a natural part of staying connected, while being specific about why the always-on nature of these apps is different from sharing location for a specific occasion. Also talk about the social pressure dimension — give your child language and permission to opt out of location sharing with contacts they are less certain about, without feeling that doing so is socially unacceptable.
🔍
Check for Successor Apps and Review Location Permissions Across All Social Apps
If your child used Zenly, check whether they have migrated to a successor application — common alternatives include Life360 (which has both a consumer social version and a family safety version), Snapchat's Snap Map, Beacon, and other apps in this category. Also review the location permissions settings on your child's device for every social app they use: many platforms request always-on location access that enables location sharing features even if a dedicated location app is not installed. Revoking always-on location permissions for social apps that do not require them — and restricting location access to only when the app is in use — is a practical step that reduces passive location exposure across all platforms simultaneously.

Use iLimits to Restrict Location-Sharing Apps Across the Category, Not Just by Individual App Name
